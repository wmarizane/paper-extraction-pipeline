## 1. Introduction 

Due to the unique cyclic topological structure and the absence of “chain ends”, cyclic polymers show some interesting properties compared to their linear counterparts, including higher refractive index and mass density [1e3], smaller hydrodynamic volume and e radius of gyration [4,5], reduced viscosity [6 9], and increased rate of crystallization [10,11]. The ring-expansion polymerization [12e17] and ring-closure methods [18e26] are the two main strategies to prepare cyclic polymers. The big obstacle among these strategies is the contamination of linear counterparts in the cyclics, which hinders the mass production of cyclic polymers since a tedious purification process is needed in order to remove the linear byproducts, as it is well-known that the cyclic polymers' properties are affected greatly by the contamination of a small portion of

## linear counterparts [27e31]. 

Recently, Laurent and Grayson [21] developed a facile method to synthesize cyclic polymers, where living/controlled polymerization was used to synthesize the linear polymers, followed with the Cucatalyzed azide/alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) [32,33] intra-chain “click” reaction to synthesize monocyclic polymers after the chain-end transformation of the corresponding linear precursors. The strategy is widely accepted and a variety of cyclic polymers e have been synthesized [34 40]. However, few publications on click cyclization have focused on a detailed quantification of cyclic purity, but that purity is a critical issue for the future of this field [29]. 

Alternatively, we envisage that the quantitative synthesis of well-defined cyclic polymers would help us avoid the contamination of linear polymers and the purification process, and open out the way to the mass production of cyclic polymers. In principle, there are three major questions hindering quantitative synthesis of cyclic polymers: 1) Can we achieve fully a,u-functionalized linear polymer precursors? 2) Can we achieve high coupling reaction efficiency (~100%) between the functional end-groups? 3) Can we

avoid the inter-chain coupling side reactions between a,u-functionalized linear polymers? Despite extensive studies on the synthesis of cyclic polymers, the above three questions have not been sufficiently addressed, and the quantitative synthesis of cyclic polymer has not been reported. 

In this work, we report our approach towards the quantitative synthesis of monocyclic polymers based on the atom transfer radical polymerization (ATRP) [41e44] and CuAAC reaction. The synthetic route is presented in Scheme 1. Styrene is selected as monomer since its ATRP process is easy to be controlled and polystyrenes (PS) are easy to characterize. All the three aforementioned questions are addressed. Namely, to achieve fully a,ufunctionalized linear polymer precursors, the conversions of linear PS (l-PS) synthesis by ATRP are controlled to be less than 20% to minimize the termination process [45]; the ring-closure “click” reaction was utilized to achieve high coupling efficiency; and the reaction was carried out in dilute conditions to suppress the interchain coupling reaction. The effects of solvents, concentration and molecular weights of l-PS on the monocyclic PS yield were studied, and the optimum conditions for the synthesis of monocyclic PS was revealed.

After that, the solution was cooled, diluted with THF and passed through a column of neutral alumina to remove the metal salt. After precipitation into a large excess of methanol, the white polymer was collected by filtration, dried at 40 C under vacuum overnight and characterized by size exclusion chromatography (SEC). Lutz et al. reported highly end-functionalized polymers can be obtained by stopping the ATRP at low conversion [51,52]. The CuAAC reaction of l-PS-N3 with different molecular weights was carried in DMF solution first with concentration of 7.9 mmol/L, which is selected to be slightly lower than the theoretical value to achieve the quantitative yields. The SEC curves of the corresponding products (c-PS-D1) are shown in Fig. 1. Clearly, the elution volume increased after reaction, indicating the formation of cyclic polymers since they have smaller hydrodynamic volumes than the corresponding linear precursors. For c-PS4k-D1 and c-PS9k-D1, only a smooth symmetric SEC curve was observed without any shoulder peak, suggesting the nearly quantitative formation of monocyclic PS as no inter-chain coupling products was observed. For c-PS14k-D1, a tiny shoulder peak was observed at the left side of main peak, which is about 1% of the main peak area calculated by Origin's Fit Multi-Peaks software analysis. This is from the interchain coupling products, which is reasonable based on Eq. (3) considering the larger molecular weight of l-PS14k-N3. The problem can be solved by carrying out the CuAAC reaction at lower concentrations. 

Fig. S4A is the SEC curve of the product from the cyclization of l- PS14k-N3 at concentration of 6.0 mmol/L in DMF. Only the curve for monocyclic PS is observed while the shoulder peak is suppressed, and the narrow unimodal curve is superimposed with the main curve of c-PS14k-D1, indicating the nearly quantitative yield of monocyclic PS. For comparison, similar reaction conditions were used for the cyclization of l-PS9k-N3, and the product's SEC curve is shown in Fig. S4B. The narrow unimodal curve is superimposed with c-PS9k-D1, confirming the nearly quantitative synthesis of monocyclic PS when the cyclization reaction of l-PS-N3 was carried at lower concentrations. 

It should be noted that these cyclic products, like previous reported materials using the click cyclization, were characterized without further purification, i.e., no azide resin treatment or SEC fractionation or HPLC fractionation of the obtained product was carried after the synthesis of cyclic polymers [48,58e60]. Thus when a nearly quantitative yield is achieved, no purification process is needed. However, the “quantitative” yield here doesn't mean the absolutely 100% conversion of linear precursors to monocyclic polymers, but near 100% conversion (>99%) that the products from side reactions (inter-chain coupling) can't be detected by SEC characterization. 

The high conversion of l-PS-N3 to monocyclic products is confirmed by the quantitative NMR spectra, where c-PS4k-D1 is presented in Fig. 2C, while c-PS9k-D1 in Fig. S1C and c-PS14k-D1 in Fig. S2C. Compared to l-PS-N3, the peaks at 4.0 ppm corresponding to methylene group adjacent to alkynyl group (Hb1, at a chain end position) and methine group connected with azide and phenyl group (Hg1, at u chain end position) disappeared, while two peaks at 4.5 ppm and 5.0 ppm assigned to the methylene (Hb2) and methine groups (Hg2) connected with the triazole ring appeared. For c-PS4k-1, the peak integration ratio of Hb2 to Hg2 is 2.00:0.97, in agreement with the theoretical value (2:1), indicating the complete transformation of alkynyl and azide groups to triazole groups. This is also supported by the IR spectra of l-PS4k-N3 and c-PS4k-1 (Fig. S5), where the bands at 2110 cm[�][1] and 3295 cm[�][1] corresponding to the absorption of azide and alkynyl groups disappeared completely after cyclization. In addition, the peak integration ratio of Hb2 to phenyl groups (Hh) is 2.00:188, from which the calculated molecular weight is 4080 Da, in accordance with the value for l- PS4k-N3 (4180 Da). Similar results were observed in the spectra of c-PS9k-D1 and c-PS14k-D1 (Table S2). Combined with the SEC results, the similar molecular weights but larger retention volumes of c-PS-D1 to their l-PS-N3 precursors clearly indicate the nearly quantitative formation of monocyclic polymers.

As pointed above, the synthesis of fully a,u-functionalized linear polymers is one of the concerns in the quantitative synthesis of cyclic polymers, and was achieved by carrying the ATRP in low yield (<20%) in our experiments. To prove this point, a control experiment was carried out, where the PS sample (l-PS-Br-ct) was synthesized at 36% yield with molecular weights of 6730 Da and PDI of 1.11. The same reaction procedure and condition for the nearly quantitative synthesis of c-PS-D1 was applied. Fig. S8 is the SEC curves for this sample before and after cyclization. After cyclization, there is a small shoulder peak remaining at the left side of main peak, which can be ascribed to the linear PS with “dead” ends. This control experiment proves the importance in controlling the monomer conversion to achieve highly a,u-functionalized linear polymers, and opens out a feasible ATRP process to them.

Quite astonishingly, for PS precursors with different molecular weights, the one with the lowest molecular weights, i.e., l-PS4k-N3, showed lower yields than l-PS9k-N3, where l-PS9k-N3 provided the highest yield of monocyclic PS in toluene. The phenomena can be explained by the “wormlike” chain configuration of PS in solution [63,64]. When the PS chains are too short, the chains behave like semi-rigid chains than the expected flexible chains. Thus the chance of intra-chain head-to-tail “biting” is smaller. [65], where the data are plotted in Fig. S13. A change of slope is observed at a critical value with the molecular weight around 4000 Da. For PS with higher molecular weights, the slope is 0.59 in good solvent and 0.51 in theta solvent, following the order for traditional flexible chains [4,66]. Eq. (3) indicates the yield of monocyclic polymer is only affected by the molar concentration of polymer, and the mean-square endto-end distance of the linear precursor. ## 4. Conclusion 

In summary, the effect of concentration, solvent and molecular weight on the synthesis of monocyclic PS (c-PS) by CuAAC method using fully a-alkynyl-u-azido functionalized PS as linear precursors was studied. The results revealed the yield of monocyclic PS decreased with increasing PS precursor concentrations as predicted. Quite astonished, the polymer chain conformation and mobility showed significant effect on the c-PS yield, where the polar good solvent DMF gave the best yield, and the PS with medium molecular weights among the three studied samples showed the highest c-PS yield. By addressing the three concerns related to the intra-chain coupling of linear precursors, a nearly quantitative synthetic approach for c-PS was established with the best yield of >99% by SEC and 98% by LCCC, at concentrations around 7.9 mmol/L or lower in DMF solution. This synthetic method excludes almost the contamination of linear polymers in the cyclic products, and opens out a promising method for mass production of cyclic polymers with high purity. Since this is at the scope of synthetic approach by living/controlled free radical polymerization, it would be a promising area in the near future.
