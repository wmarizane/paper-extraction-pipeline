## **1. Introduction** 

Thermoplastic polyurethanes (TPUs) are segmented multiblock copolymers containing alternating soft segment (SS) and hard segment (HS). Due to their biocompatibility they have been extensively employed as biomaterials. Most commercially available TPUs use low molecular weight (MW) polyester or polyether diols as the SS. However, these TPUs are unstable against hydrolytic or oxidative degradations, which limit their in vivo lifetime. To overcome this problem, we recently introduced polyisobutylene (PIB) based TPUs, which exhibited enhanced biostability against oxidative degradation. [1] The precursor PIB-diol can be prepared by living cationic polymerization followed by endfunctionalization. One well known method shown in Fig. 1 employs hydroboration–oxidation of allyl-telechelic PIB obtained by reacting living PIB with allyltrimethylsilane. For the synthesis of TPUs, the number average functionality (Fn) of PIB-diol should be close to the ideal value of 2.0, since the number average degree of extension and thus the ultimate number average molecular weight (Mn)

of the TPU is very sensitive to Fn. [2] To obtain high molecular weight is highly desirable since the ultimate tensile properties depend very strongly on molecular weight. [3] Due to incomplete functionalization and/or side reactions, some monofunctional and non-functional PIB may form along with PIB-diol (Fig. 1). [4] Thus, it is necessary to confirm the chemical homogeneity of PIB-diol before using it for the synthesis of TPU. Normal characterization methods, such as NMR and FT-IR spectroscopy are generally unsuitable to determine the existence of a small amount of undesired side product(s). Again, these techniques cannot distinguish polymer chains with different functionalities. Previous researchers have tried to utilize size exclusion chromatography (SEC) for the determination of chemical heterogeneity of polymers [5] and copolymers. [6] However, SEC can only provide limited information on the chemical composition of a polymer, if only interfaced with NMR [7] or mass spectrometry. [8] Subsequently, liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) in a gradient mode was developed. [9] This technique, when applied to separate polymers in terms of their chemical heterogeneity, often suffers from complications arising from the MW dependency of polymer retention and/or precipitation/dissolution of the polymer. [10] Liquid chromatography at critical conditions of adsorption (LCCC), established by Belinki et al. [11] and Entelis et al., [12] is

capable of separating polymers according to their chemical functionalities (such as the number and types of functional groups and their positions in the polymer chain), independently of their MW and determine the chemical heterogeneities. [13] LCCC conditions are in between the condition of SEC, where separation is based on the entropic differences between the macromolecules according to their hydrodynamic volumes and LAC, where separation is based on the enthalpic differences between the macromolecules. [14] At this critical condition, there is a balance between the entropic and enthalpic interactions leading to the exclusive interaction of a single functional group (e.g., end group of the polymer chain) with the stationary phase and separation of macromolecules according to the end group functionality, independently of their MW. [15] Critical conditions are strongly related to the polymer under consideration and thus must be individually determined for every different polymer. [16] There are two practical limitations of LCCC: (a) The development of LCCC method is time-consuming to find the proper eluent composition to reach at the LCCC condition. (b) The most important limitation of LCCC of polymers is reduced sample recovery, especially in case of high molecular weight polymers. This phenomenon is well documented in the literature devoted to LCCC method development. [17] This incomplete polymer elution can sufficiently reduce both precision and accuracy of the quantitative LCCC analyses [18] and may cause a change in the column retentivity in the course of large series of LCCC measurements. [19] Matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionization-time-of-flight mass spectrometry (MALDI-TOF MS) provides valuable information on the polymer end group structure. [20] The information obtained from MALDI-TOF MS analysis can be a strong supplementary tool in combination with LCCC for characterization of a polymer. [21] In the present contribution, we focus on developing a LCCC method using RI detector as a concentration detector for the determination of chemical heterogeneity and analysis by gradient HPLC and MALDI-TOF MS to identify the structure of monofunctional and non-functional PIB in the synthesized PIB-diol sample. PIB-diol, an important precursor for TPU synthesis, is not easy to characterize by the described technologies. ## **3. Results** 

## **and discussion** Analysis by gradient HPLC using UV detector 

PIBOL014 was first analyzed by a solvent gradient HPLC method based on UV detector using heptane/MTBE as the eluent. Non-functional PIB eluted first, well separated from PIB-monool, which was followed by PIB-diol (Fig. 2). The non-functional PIB eluted first as it has no interaction with the column. PIB-monool eluted before PIB-diol as it has less interaction with the column in comparison with PIB-diol species with two hydroxyl groups. However, the separation between the PIB-monool and diol was less clear as high MW (to be explained later) PIB-diol eluted with PIB-monool. The reason why we used ELSD for subsequent analysis instead of UV has nothing to do with the concern of different absorptivity of the PIB species present in PIBOL014. It was simply 

because UV absorptivity of the PIB sample was too low and ELSD provides a much higher sensitivity. 3.2. Analysis by gradient HPLC using ELSD and MALDI-TOF MS 

In a modified solvent gradient method with ELS detection using heptane/MTBE as the eluent an isocratic flow region of heptane/MTBE 91/9 (w/w) from t = 5 min to t = 25 min was inserted between two gradient regions. HPLC chromatogram of PIBOL014 (Fig. 3) under this condition revealed three well separated peaks, which correspond to non-functional PIB, PIB-monool and PIB-diol, with PIB-diol as the major component. The amount of each PIB fraction was calculated by calculating the area under the peak, as shown in Fig. 3. 

To identify the end group structures, the four fractions i.e., nonfunctional PIB (f1), PIB-monool (f2) and two fractions of PIB-diol (f3, f4) indicated on Fig. 3 were analyzed by MALDI-TOF MS (Fig. 4). Fig. 5 shows the expanded MALDI-TOF MS spectra of the four fractions. 3.3. LCCC experiments: determination of critical conditions of adsorption 

To determine the critical condition of adsorption, PIB-diol samples of different MW (approximately 1000, 2000 and 5000) and PIB-diallyl samples of different MW (approximately 1000, 2000, 5000, 10,000 and 21,000) were analyzed at different ratios of THF and methanol. According to Fig. 6 in THF/methanol 80.5/19.5 (w/w) PIB samples with identical chemical functionality eluted at the same position, independently of their MW. The same THF/methanol 80.5/19.5 (w/w) composition has already been employed by Mueller and coworkers for the LCCC analysis of PIB block copolymers. [26] However, this solvent composition is not suitable for high MW PIB as suggested by the very low sample recovery observed with Mn = 20900 due to precipitation of the polymer. 

## 3.4. LCCC experiments: analysis at critical conditions using ELS detector 

LCCC chromatograms of PIB samples of different functionality but similar MW (approximately 2000) under critical condition using ELS detector are shown as Fig. 7. PIB samples of the same MW (namely PIB-monool, PIB-diol, PIB-diallyl and PIB-dichloride) eluted at different elution time, depending on their chemical functionality under LCCC condition. As expected bifunctional PIB-diol eluted first, well separated from PIB-monool followed by nonfunctional PIB-diallyl (Fig. 7). However, when mixed PIB-diol and PIB-diallyl was injected, the ratio of PIB-diol and PIB-diallyl calculated from the area under the peak (Fig. 8) was slightly different from the actual ratio in the sample. Similarly, PIB-monool mixed with PIB-diol yielded two distinct peaks but the calculated ratio

## 3.5. LCCC experiments: analysis at critical conditions using RI detector 

Fig. 9 depicts LCCC chromatograms of PIB samples of different functionality but similar MW (approximately 2000) under critical condition using RI detector as the concentration detector. PIB samples (namely PIB-monool, PIB-diol, PIB-diallyl and PIB-dichloride) of the same MW eluted at different elution time depending on their chemical functionality under LCCC condition. PIB-diol eluted earlier compared to PIB-monool, while PIB-diallyl and exo olefin difunctional PIB (PIB-diolefin) eluted at a higher elution time compared to PIB-monool. Now, to check whether the LCCC-RI signal is proportional with the concentration of the PIB species or not, we mixed PIB-diol/PIB-diallyl (Fig. 10A) and PIBdiol/PIB-monool (Fig. 10B) at 50:50 ratio and injected them into the LCCC-RI system. Results revealed that the LCCC-RI signal is proportional to the concentration of the PIB species present in the mixture (Fig. 10). Thus, RI detector emerged as a better concentration detector under LCCC condition. The shift of the elution volume is due to the difference in kind of detection. RI detector has a large internal volume due to tubing inside the RI detector that the sample has to travel through. Therefore the RI detector will have a longer delay in retention time compared to the ELS detector. 

The LCCC chromatogram of PIBOL014 revealed three peaks (Fig. 11) with elution times correlating to PIB-diol, PIB-monool and PIB without ~~O~~ H functionality. Since according to MALDI-TOF MS, PIBOL014 does not contain PIB-diallyl this peak is attributed to PIB-diolefin, which may have formed from PIB-dichloride by elimination of HCl upon standing or during polymerization/end functionalization.
