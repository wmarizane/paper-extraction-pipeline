## **1. Introduction** 

Polyoxyalkylene block copolymers are a versatile class of polymers. Depending on the substituent on the oxirane ring, these polymers show very different solution behaviour. Polyoxyethylenes (without any substituent) are very hydrophilic, while polymers with methyl, ethyl and butyl substituent are more lipophilic in the same order. Hence polyoxyalkylene block copolymers of ethylene oxide (EO) and higher alkylene oxides (e.g. propylene oxide (PO), butene oxide (BO) and hexene oxide (HO)) are amphiphilic in nature. These type of copolymers were developed and commercialized some 50 years ago and are still being used in many different fields like cosmetics, medicines, cleaners, lubricants, and many others [1]. Poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) is used as hydrophilic block while polymers of higher alkylene oxides like poly(propylene oxide) (PPO), poly(butene oxide) (PBO) and poly(hexene oxide) (PHO) are used as hydrophobic moiety. Most commercialized products of this class are poloxamers (PEO–PPO–PEO) [2,3–16]. There are also many reports on EO–PO diblock copolymers [1,5,17–20], but literature is very limited on

As polyoxyalkylene block copolymers are often synthesized by anionic ring opening polymerization of alkylene oxides, one has to take into account possible side reactions leading to the formation of homopolymers. In the presence of water polyalkylene glycols may be formed [21,23], which can be avoided by drying the starting materials carefully. Another side reaction can, however, not completely be avoided: chain transfer to the monomer leads to homopolymers with unsaturated end groups. This reaction has mainly been studied for PO [20,27–29], but it may also happen in the polymerization of higher alkylene oxides. One may speculate that it may be less important in this case because of steric hindrance on the methylene group next to the oxirane ring. 

The lengths of homopolymer diol, homopolymer with unsaturated end group and higher alkylene oxide block in block copolymer may be very different. One may expect that the diol has the highest molar mass, as it may grow in both directions, and starts to grow at the beginning of the polymerization. The unsaturated homopolymer, however, must have a much lower molar mass, as the chain transfer happens all the time, consequently some new chains will still be started at rather high conversion of the monomer.

## **2. Experimental** 

Higher alkylene oxides (butene oxide and hexene oxide) and polyethylene glycol monomethyl ethers (MeO-PEO) with different molecular weights (550, 1100, 2000) were purchased from Fluka (Buchs, Switzerland) and sodium hydride (60% dispersion in mineral oil) from Aldrich. MeO-PEO was dried by azeotropic distillation with toluene. Alkylene oxides were refluxed over Calcium hydride (Fluka). 

Block copolymers were synthesized by microwave-assisted anionic ring opening polymerizations of higher alkylene oxides (butene oxide, hexene oxide) using MeO-PEO as initiator and sodium hydride as co-initiator according to the literature [24,25]. Briefly all reagents (MeO-PEO, alkylene oxide monomer and sodium hydride) were taken in 2–5 mL (filling volume) microwave process vials (Biotage AB) and sealed microwave vials were introduced into the cavity of single-mode microwave reactor (Biotage Initiator Eight EXP 2.0, absorbance level: high). Polymerizations were carried out at 160[◦] C till complete conversion. After polymerization, equimolar quantity of acetic acid was added to neutralize the active chain end. 

All separations were performed using the density detection system DDS 70 (CHROMTECH, Graz, Austria) which has been developed by our group. Data acquisition and processing was carried out by using software package CHROMA. 

In the semipreparative LCCC system, which was used in the first dimension, the mobile phase was delivered by JASCO 880 PU pump (Japan Spectrosopic Company, Tokyo, Japan) at a flow rate of 2.0 mL min[−][1] . Samples were injected manually using Rheodyne 7125 injection valve (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA) equipped with a 500 �L loop. A semi-preparative column was used for all measurements: Spherisorb ODS2 (250 mm × 10 mm, 5 �m, 80 Å) from PhaseSep (Deeside, UK). A SICON LCD 201 RI detector was combined with the DDS70. Fractions were collected using an Advantec SF 2120 fraction collector (Advantec, Dublin, CA, USA). Critical conditions for PBO were realized in 88 wt% THF–water and for PHO in 92 wt% THF–water. 

The solvent was removed from the fractions by freeze drying using a Benchtop 2K ES (Virits, USA). The limits of the instrument are −60[◦] C and 2.6 Pa. Samples were frozen to about −40[◦] C and were subjected for drying to benchtop 2K ES which was premaintained at a temperature below −50[◦] C and a pressure below 3 Pa. Typically it took 2–3 h for complete drying of the samples. 

In the SEC system (second dimension), we used a modular system consisting of a Gynkotek 300C pump, a VICI injector equipped with a 100 �L sample loop, two column selection valves Rheodyne 7060 (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA), a density detec- 

tion system DDS 70 (Chromtech, Graz, Austria) coupled with a Bischoff 8110 refractive index detector (Bischoff, Leonberg, Germany). SEC measurements were performed on a 7.8 mm × 600 mm Phenogel 100 Å column (Phenomenex, Torrance, CA, USA) or a 7.8 mm × 300 mm PLgel MIXED E column (Polymer Laboratories, Church Stretton, Shropshire, UK) in chloroform at a flow-rate of 1.00 mL min[−][1] . Sample concentrations were 3.0–10.0 g L[−][1] . The columns were calibrated with PPGs from Sigma–Aldrich and FLUKA (Buchs, Switzerland). Molar mass distributions were calculated using CHROMA. 

In the analytical LCCC system (second dimension), the mobile phase was delivered by an ISCO 2350 HPLC pump (ISCO, Lincoln, NE, USA) at a flow rate of 0.5 mL min[−][1] . Samples were injected manually using a Rheodyne 7125 injection valve (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA) equipped with a 50 �L loop. A refractive index detector RI 2414 (Waters) or a SEDEX 45 ELSD (Sedere, France) was connected to the DDS 70. Nitrogen was used as carrier gas, and the pressure at the nebulizer was set to 1.0 bar (evaporator temperature: 30[◦] C). Normal phase column used for LCCC in second dimension was Nucleosil 100-5 OH 5 �m, silica-based diol phase; 4.6 mm × 250 mm; particle diameter: 5 �m; pore diameter: 100 Å (Macherey-Nagel, Dueren, Germany). Critical conditions for PEO exist at 75.7 wt% acetone–water. 

In LAC, the separations in the second dimension were performed on a gradient system Ultimate 3000, consisting of a pump DGP3600A, solvent degasser SRD-3600, column thermostat TCC-3000, autosampler WPS-3000SL, all from Dionex (Germerink, Germany), an evaporative light scattering detector PL-ELS 2100 (Polymer Laboratories, Church Stretton, Shropshire, UK). Data acquisition and processing was performed using the software Chromeleon (Dionex, Germerink, Germany). Gradient separations were performed on a Nucleosil 4000-7 C18 (Macherey-Nagel, Germany): silica-based octadecyl phase; 4 mm × 250 mm; particle diameter = 7 �m; pore size = 4000 Å at a flow rate of 0.5 mL min[−][1] . The injected sample volumes depended on the total volume of the fraction from the first dimension in order to inject comparable sample sizes. 

The following gradient was used: start 80 vol% methanol–water, in 10 min to 90 vol% methanol, in 30 min 95 vol% methanol, in 40 min 98 vol% methanol, 15 min constant at 98 vol%, then back to 80 vol% methanol in 1 min and 4 min at 80 vol% methanol. 

The solvents for HPLC (THF, chloroform, acetone and water, all HPLC grade) were purchased from Roth (Karlsruhe, Germany). 

Mobile phases for LCCC (both for analytical and preperative scale) were mixed by mass and vacuum degassed, their composition was controlled by density measurement using a DMA 60 density meter equipped with a measuring cell DMA 602M (A. Paar, Graz, Austria). 

The columns and density cells in all systems were placed in thermostatted boxes, in which a temperature (25.0[◦] C for LCCC and at 30.0[◦] C for SEC) was maintained for all measurements using a thermostat Lauda RM6, Lauda-Königshofen, Germany). 

MALDI-TOF Mass Spectrometry was performed on a Micromass TofSpec 2E Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometer. The instrument is equipped with a nitrogen laser (337 nm wavelength, operated at a frequency of 5 Hz), and a time lag focusing unit. Ions were generated by irradiation slightly above the threshold laser power. Positive ion spectra were recorded in reflectron mode applying an accelerating voltage of 20 kV and externally calibrated with a suitable mixture of poly(ethyleneglycol)s (PEO). The spectra of 100–150 shots were averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio. Analysis of data was done with MassLynx-Software V3.5 (Micromass/Waters, Manchester, UK). 

Sample were prepared by mixing a solution of dithranol (c = 10 mg mL[−][1] in THF), a solution of the analyte (c = 2 mg mL[−][1] in THF), and a solution of CF3COONa (NaTFA; c = 0.1 mg mL[−][1] in THF), in a ratio of 7:1:1 (v/v/v). 0.5 �L of the resulting mixture were

## **3. Results and discussion** 

## 3.1. HPLC of polymers 

Functional polymers and oligomers can be characterized by using different chromatographic techniques, which separate according to different criteria. The type of chromatographic separation is determined by the so-called interaction parameter c, which describes the interaction of a structural unit with the stationary phase [46]. 

This parameter is negative in size exclusion chromatography (SEC) and positive in liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC). At the critical adsorption point (CAP) the interaction parameter equals zero, and the corresponding block becomes “chromatographically invisible” [47–50]. This effect is utilized in liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC), which allows the separation of a polymer homologous series according to their end groups [48,51–53]. 

In the case of amphiphilic polymers the interaction parameters cA and cB of the individual structural units (A and B) may have different values. If both cA and cB are negative, the molar mass distribution (MMD) can be determined by size exclusion chromatography (SEC), with retention decreasing with increasing molar mass, and only a minor influence of chemical composition. SEC yields the overall MMD, with dual detection [54–56] one may obtain information on the chemical composition along the MMD [56–58]. SEC is, however, not capable of discriminating a mixture of block copolymers and homopolymers. In the case of higher polymers, gradient LAC [59–66] or LC under limiting conditions [67–69] can be applied. In the range of lower molecular mass, however, LCCC is the best approach to separate homopolymers from the block copolymer. In AB diblock copolymers (e.g. A = EO and B = PO or another alkylene oxide) one may use the following strategies: At the CAP for the unit A, a separation according to the block B may be achieved by a SEC or a LAC mechanism, while at the CAP for the block B, the length of the block A may be obtained in the same way. The application of these methods to EO–PO block copolymers has been described recently [20,45,70–72].

## 3.6. LAC in second dimension 

Liquid adsorption chromatography is powerful technique for the separation of oligomers of low molecular weight samples. Using methanol–water as mobile phase on a octadecyl RP columns, there exists a CAP for EO for a wide range of mobile phase composition (75–95% methanol). In the range mentioned above EO is fairly critical. Consequently, a gradient (see Section 2) was run in this range to achieve the separation of according oligomers of BO. It is clear from Fig. 9 that the raw sample contained two series of oligomers, one with quite low molecular weight while second had higher molecular weight. From the LAC-trace of fraction 1 (from Fig. 3) it can be deduced that this is indeed a block copolymer with comparatively higher molecular weight. Fraction 2 (from Fig. 3) exhibited a very broad distribution having a low molecular maximum and a second maximum at higher molecular weight. The lower molecular weight part of fraction 2 has two series. One is assigned to diols while the other is interpreted as a series of oligomers with unsaturated end groups. The higher molecular weight part of fraction 2 contained only one series (results are similar to that of spectroscopy, see below). This may be due to different sources of side reactions 

as explained in our previous paper [45]. The evaporative light scattering detector (ELSD) was used for LAC in second dimension. This detector can only detect non-volatile component of analyte. As discussed above the conditions used for LAC separation are critical for PEO. Therefore if there is some unreacted initiator is present in the sample, it would elute at the void volume of column (which should not give any signal by ELSD otherwise). It can be noted that there are some traces of unreacted MeO-PEO in the sample that were not detected by LCCC on normal phase column in second dimension. This agrees quite well with the results from spectroscopy. 

For the block copolymers containing polyhexene oxide as hydrophobic moiety, such a separation was not possible since the interaction parameter of hexene oxide in such a mobile phase composition is too high. Experiments to find a system with CAP for EO and reasonable interaction parameter for hexene oxide (2–3) with good solubility were not successful until yet.
