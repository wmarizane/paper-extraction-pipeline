## Full separation of oligomers in block copolymers of ethylene oxide and propylene oxide 

In this study di- and triblock copolymers of ethylene oxide (EO) and propylene oxide (PO) are analyzed by using CAP for one block and adsorption for the other block. This gives a complete picture of EO- and PO-based block copolymer with respect to the oligomers of both blocks. A full resolution of the individual oligomers can be achieved for both blocks up to an average molecular weight of 1000–1500 of each block. 

Keywords: Amphiphilic block copolymers / Critical adsorption point (CAP) / Liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC) / Oligomers

## 1 Introduction 

Amphiphilic block copolymers are a very important group of modern polymeric materials. Along with applications in many different fields like nano- and biomaterials [1], modification of surface to control protein adsorption [1], for controlled release [2], for nanofiltration membranes, as synthetic lubricants and emulsifiers and for biomedical applications; these fascinating materials bring many new challenges to polymer chemists and technologists [3, 4]. 

An especially important class of block copolymers are block copolymers of ethylene oxide (EO) and propylene oxide (PO), which have a very wide range of applications [4]. 

Basically, such materials can consist of two or three blocks. In the triblocks, the blocks may be arranged in a different way: the hydrophilic block (EO) may form the center of the chain or the arms. In the following text, we shall use the abbreviations EO–PO for the diblock copolymer and EO–PO–EO or PO–EO–PO for the triblock copolymers. 

The diblocks are typically synthesized by propoxylation of monoethers of PEG, while the triblocks can be obtained by ethoxylation of polypropylene glycol or by propoxylation of PEG. As possible side products, the tri-

blocks may contain the homopolymers as well as the diblock copolymer. 

Obviously, the physical properties of the block copolymers are strongly influenced by the distributions of molar mass, functionality, chemical composition, architecture, and content of the side products [5]. Consequently there is a strong need for reliable analytical methods, which can provide the required information in order to improve the synthetic procedures to yield well defined products of high purity. 

Basically, one may apply different modes of LC and spectroscopic techniques (such as NMR and MALDI-TOFMS), and combinations of these. NMR alone (without any separation) yields only average values. MALDI-TOF-MS is problematic with highly polydisperse samples. When applied to fractions from a chromatographic separation, which have rather narrow distributions, it is, however, a very powerful technique. 

The most popular chromatographic technique in polymer analysis is size exclusion chromatography (SEC), which yields the molar mass distribution (MMD) [6, 7]. SEC with dual detection [7–13] yields information on the chemical composition along the MMD. It must, however, be mentioned, that SEC cannot differentiate between a block copolymer and a mixture of homopolymers. 

This can, however, be achieved by using LC under critical conditions (LCCC) [14–24]. At a special temperature and in a special mobile phase composition, retention is independent of the molar mass of a polymer chain or one block; which thus becomes “chromatographically invisible”. This situation is called the critical adsorption point (CAP). Under these conditions, one may separate a block copolymer from a homopolymer. At the CAP for one block, the elution volume depends on the chemical nature and the size of the other block, which may be

In the first case it elutes in the SEC regime (i.e., earlier than the critical homopolymer, which elutes at the void volume of the column), in the latter case it will elute in the regime of liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC), i.e., much later then the critical homopolymer [17, 24–26]. 

In the analysis of block copolymers, the first situation is commonly used [8–11, 24], while the second one is typically applied in the separation of functional polymers [8, 10]. Obviously, SEC for the noncritical block will work for any size of this block, provided that it is large enough to be separated from the solvent peak. 

LAC of the noncritical block works only in a limited range of molar mass, as retention increases exponentially with the size of this block. At the mobile phase composition corresponding to the CAP for the first block retention may be too strong, if the adsorbed block is long. In mobile phases consisting of different solvents, the retention of one block at the CAP for the other one may be different [17]. A fine adjustment can be achieved by changing the temperature and the pore size of the column [17, 27, 28]: columns with the same chemical nature, but different pore size will have a CAP at the same mobile phase composition. As retention in LAC depends on the pore surface (and thus on the pore diameter), one may separate copolymers of different molar mass on columns with different porosity [27–30]. 

In the case of EO- and PO-based block copolymers, the approach with an excluded noncritical block can be applied both to EO–PO–EO and PO–EO–PO triblocks [24]. 

The situation with adsorbed noncritical blocks works quite well for diblocks, for triblocks it is, however, much more complicated, as will be discussed later on. In a triblock, it is only possible to make the outer block invisible and to separate the inner block by LAC, while at the CAP for the inner block retention is not independent of its length [31, 32]. This means, that in 2-D separations it makes a difference, which mechanism is used in the first and the second dimension. 

In principle, LAC for PO at the CAP for EO can be achieved in RP systems [17, 25, 26]. 

LAC for EO at the CAP for PO should be possible on normal phase systems. Such separations have not yet been described in the literature. 

In this study, we have tried to find a system, which allows an LAC separation according to the number of EO units at the CAP for PO.

## 4 Results and discussion 

## 4.1 Reversed phases 

In the literature, critical conditions of PO have been described on C18 columns and EO elutes in exclusion regime in almost all cases [24, 25, 48]. 

Critical conditions for EO have been observed on RP columns in methanol–water (with 80–90 wt% methanol) [5, 49] and acetone–water (with 25–30 wt% acetone) [31, 32]. In such mobile phases, PO elutes in the LAC mode [17, 30]. In acetone–water one may separate only very low oligomers of PO, as the interaction parameter of PO is too large. In methanol–water, a separation of longer PO chains can be achieved. We have now tried to find a CAP for EO in another mobile phase, which would allow a better separation according to the number of PO units. 

A separation according to the number of PO unit by LAC under critical conditions for EO has also been described on RP columns in ACN–water [26]. For comparison, we have also determined the CAP on different RP columns in ACN–water. 

As can be seen in Fig. 1, critical conditions were found on different columns at about 34 wt% ACN. It is also shown in the same figure that CAP for EO on such RP columns is not a sharp point but more or less a critical adsorption range (34–59% ACN). A similar behavior has already been observed on RP columns in other mobile phases: while in acetone–water there is a sharp critical point for PEG, in methanol–water a similar plateau is

## 5 Conclusions 

EO–PO diblock copolymers are analyzed by using LCCC. In this report, we used CAP for one block and adsorption for the second block. Depending on the pore diameter of the column used, all oligomers with respect to both blocks can be resolved within reasonable range of molecular weight (around 1000–1500 for each block).
