Amphiphilic block copolymers were synthesised by ring opening polymerization of e-caprolactone with polyethylene glycol monomethyl ether (PEG-MME) using different catalysts (boron trifluoride, sodium hydride, and tin octoate) in a one pot procedure. The products obtained were characterized with respect to their molar mass distribution and content of homopolymers using size exclusion chromatography (SEC), Liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC) and MALDI-TOF–MS. The homopolymers of caprolactone could be separated from the block copolymer by LCCC on a reversed phase column in tetrahydrofurane–water mobile phases with evaporative light scattering detection (ELSD). Residual monomer could be determined under the same conditions using density detection, a separation of the copolymer from residual initiator could be achieved on a normal phase column in acetone–water mobile phases.

## 1. Introduction 

Poly(e-caprolactone) (PCL) is one of the promising materials which can be applied for biomedical applications owing to its good biocompatibility, permeability and degradability [1]. Generally speaking, PCL is rather hydrophobic and it degrades very slowly by simple hydrolysis under the human body conditions. In the last years, amphiphilic block copolymers of e-caprolactone (CL) and ethylene oxide (EO) have attracted increasing attention because of their special properties [2].

In general, amphiphilic block copolymers can self-assemble to form nanosized micelles consisting of a hydrophilic outer shell and a hydrophobic inner core in aqueous media. Such micelles with core-shell structure can readily incorporate lipophilic drugs into their cores and release them in a controlled manner at a later stage, while the hydrophilic shell can provide stabilization for the micelles without the need for additional stablizers, thus making them potential carriers for drugs with poor water solubility [5].

Besides diblock copolymers, also EO–CL–EO and CL– EO–CL triblocks have been described, and in some papers also stars containing different arms consisting of EO or CL [10]. 

In the literature there are numerous papers on the polymerization of lactones, in which different conditions for the polymerization are described. The polymerization has been performed using different catalysts [11], such as HCl in diethyl ether, alkoxylates of tin, lanthanum, calcium and other metals, sometimes also with metal complexes [12]. CL was also polymerized in presence of carboxylic acids without a metal catalyst [13] in order to avoid the presence of potentially toxic metal ions in the product. 

Very often tin octoate is applied, as it has been approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) [12]. 

The polymerization has also been carried out with microwave heating, which offers a number of advantages over conventional heating such as non contact heating, instantaneous and rapid heating and highly specific heating [14]. There has been some discussion about possible microwave effects [15–17]. 

Depending on the catalyst used the polymerization may proceed through different mechanisms (cationic, anionic, or coordinative), which may lead to products containing different amounts of unwanted molecules, such as linear or cyclic homopolymers etc. 

Cyclic molecules are formed by a backbiting reaction, which can also occur without any catalyst, when PCL is heated over a longer period: the terminal hydroxy group may react with an ester group within the same chain, which leads to cyclic PCL oligomers, shorter block copolymers, and (in the extreme case) to the PEG homopolymer. Of course, there may also be intermolecular transesterification reactions. 

According to the different nature of the active chain end, different side reactions may occur; consequently products with a different purity may be obtained. 

As most of the applications mentioned above demand a high degree of purity, it appeared to be necessary to study the influence of reaction conditions (catalyst, temperature etc.) on the content of possible side products and residual starting materials. This required the development of reliable characterization methods, which yield the desired information. 

## 2. Characterization of block copolymers 

In the literature, the products are typically characterized using SEC, NMR, sometimes also by MALDI-TOF–MS. These techniques yield different information. 

Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) can be used for determination of molar mass and molar mass distribution. If the product contains residual monomer or lower oligomers of CL, which are smaller than the initiator (PEGMME), they can be determined by SEC. No information is, 

however, obtained, whether the polymer peak contains the initiator or homopolymers besides the desired block copolymer. 

NMR shows the presence of particular structural units (such as ether and ester groups) in the sample, it yields, however, no information on whether these groups are in the same or in different molecules. In other words, it yields just an average composition. 

MALDI-TOF–MS is a powerful technique to characterize polymers with respect to their repeat unit and functionality, it is, however, not quantitative. Moreover, it is problematic in the characterization of samples with large polydispersities. 

The required information – which includes the distributions of molar mass (MMD) and chemical composition (CCD) – can only be obtained by a combination of different techniques, which yield different kind of information. 

Besides SEC, other chromatographic techniques may be applied: liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) is mainly useful in the analysis of homopolymers. With block copolymers, where all blocks are polydisperse, overlapping series of peaks will be obtained. 

Such samples can, however, be analyzed using a special mode of liquid chromatography, which allows the analysis of polymers according to structural units different from the repeat unit: liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC)[18,19]. 

At a special mobile phase composition and temperature (the critical adsorption point, CAP) the retention volume of a polymer becomes independent of molar mass, which means, that a chain consisting of this particular repeat unit becomes chromatographically invisible [20,21]. 

This offers the opportunity to separate polydisperse polymers with respect to other structural units, such as end groups, other blocks etc. [22–25]. 

At the CAP for one repeat unit the corresponding (nonfunctional) homopolymer will elute at (or close to) the void volume. A polymer containing special end groups or another block can then be separated by different mechanisms. In principle, at the CAP for the main chain there may be different situations [21,26]. 

If the end groups or the other block of a copolymer interact with the surface of the stationary phase, all chains containing these structural units will elute in LAC mode (i.e. later than the homopolymer) [27,28]. This situation is useful in the separation of homopolymers according to their functionality. In the case of block copolymers, however, where the other block is polydisperse, the interaction with the stationary phase may be too strong, and thus the retention of higher oligomers may be too high. 

If the other block is excluded from the pores, all chains containing this block will elute in SEC mode, i.e. earlier than the non-functional homopolymer. In the case of block copolymers (where the individual blocks are typically polydisperse), this situation is more favourable. It allows the determination of the length of the (non-critical) block and shows the presence of the critical homopolymer. 

In the second case (one block at the CAP, the other one in SEC mode) one may separate the critical homopolymer from the copolymer (which coelutes with the excluded homopolymer)[29,30].

If critical conditions can be realized for both structural units (on different columns in different mobile phases), one my identify the homopolymers eluting at the CAP and determine the length of the excluded block. 

This could be achieved for EO–PO block copolymers, as has been shown in a previous paper [26]: at the CAP for PO on a reversed phase column EO elutes in SEC mode, while at the CAP for EO on a normal phase column PO elutes in SEC mode. By combination of these techniques, the individual block length can be determined independently. 

The situation is quite similar for EO–CL block copolymers: at the CAP for EO on the NP column one can also expect CL to elute in SEC mode. If a critical point for CL could be found on a reversed phase column, EO should also elute in SEC mode. 

## 3. Experimental 

## 3.1. Materials 

Polyethylene glycol monomethyl ethers with an average molar mass of 2000 and 5000 (PEG-MME 2000 and PEG-MME 5000) and e-caprolactone were purchased from Fluka (Buchs, Switzerland), tin(II) octoate, boron trifluoride ethyl etherate (BF3�Et2O), and NaH from Sigma–Aldrich (Vienna, Austria). The solvents used in chromatography were trichloromethane, acetone, THF, and water (all HPLC grade, from Carl Roth Gmbh, Karlsruhe, Germany). 

## 3.2. Polymerization 

e-Caprolactone was polymerized using BF3�Et2O, sodium hydride, and tin(II) octoate as catalysts and methanol, triethylene glycol monomethyl ether, PEG-MME 2000 and 5000 as initiators. All polymerizations were performed under Argon atmosphere. 

Cationic polymerization (with BF3�Et2O) was performed in dichloromethane solution at room temperature (over night). 

Anionic polymerization with sodium hydride was performed in THF solution. In this case the polymerization proceeded very rapidly: typically complete conversion was reached after a few minutes. In order to study the effect of heating on the formation of side products (by transesterification or backbiting), the reaction mixtures were also heated in microwave for up to 60 min at 140 �C. Anionic polymerizations were terminated by addition of acetic acid. 

The other polymerizations were performed in bulk: the catalyst was added to the initiator (in the case of PEG-MME in melt), then the monomer was added. With tin octoate the polymerization was performed with miocrowave heating at 140 �C for 30 min in an Emrays ‘Optimizer’ Microwave instrument. 

## 3.3. Spectroscopy 

MALDI-TOF–Mass Spectrometry was performed on a Micromass TofSpec 2E Time-of-Flight Mass Spectrometer. The instrument is equipped with a nitrogen laser (337 nm 

wavelength, operated at a frequency of 5 Hz), and a time lag focusing unit. Ions were generated by irradiation slightly above the threshold laser power. Positive ion spectra were recorded in reflectron mode applying an accelerating voltage of 20 kV and externally calibrated with a suitable mixture of poly(ethyleneglycol)s (PEG). The spectra of 100– 150 shots were averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio. Analysis of data was done with MassLynx-Software V3.5 (Micromass/Waters, Manchester, UK). 

Sample solutions were prepared by mixing a solution of dithranol (c = 10 mg/mL in THF), a solution of the analyte (c = 2 mg/mL in THF), and a solution of CF3COONa (NaTFA; c = 0.1 mg/mL in THF), in a ratio of 7:1:1 (v/v/v). 0.5 lL of the resulting mixture were deposited on the sample plate (stainless steel) and allowed to dry under air. 

## 3.4. Chromatography 

Liquid chromatography was performed using the density detection system DDS 70 (CHROMTECH, Graz, Austria) which has been developed by our group. Data acquisition and processing was carried out by using software package CHROMA. 

The solvents (chloroform, THF, acetone, methanol and water, all HPLC grade) were purchased from Roth (Karlsruhe, Germany) and Merck (Darmstadt, Germany). 

Mobile phases were mixed by mass and vacuum degassed, their composition was controlled by density measurement using a DMA 60 density meter equipped with a measuring cell DMA 602M (A. Paar, Graz, Austria). 

SEC measurements were performed on a modular SEC system comprising of a Gynkotek 300C pump (Gynkotek, Germering, Germany) equipped with a VICI injector (sample loop 100 ll)(from VICI, Baton Rouge, LA, USA), two column selection valves Rheodyne 7060 (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA), a density detection system DDS 70 (Chromtech, Graz, Austria) coupled with an ERC 7512 RI detector (ERMA, Japan). ## 4. Results and discussion 

In this study, we have synthesized diblock copolymers by polymerization of CL with PEG-MME as initiator. For comparison, homopolymers were synthesized with methanol or water as initiator. The resulting polymers have one hydroxy group at one end, while the other one is either a methyl ester or a carboxy group. In the following text we shall refer to these polymers as PCL methyl esters (PCLME) and PCL acid (PCL-A), as is shown in Scheme 1.

## 5. Conclusions 

Block copolymers of ethylene oxide and e-caprolactone can be characterized using size exclusion chromatography (SEC) and liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC). SEC yields the overall MMD of the products, the even more important information on chemical heterogeneity can be provided by LCCC. On a normal phase column, critical conditions for PEO can be realized. In such a mobile phase PCL elutes in SEC mode, which allows a determination of the length of the PCL block and the identification of unreacted PEG (if present in the polymer). On a reversed phase column, critical conditions for CL have been found. Under such conditions, PEO elutes in SEC mode, which allows a determination of the length of the EO block, and the identification of CL homopolymers in the block copolymer. 

Moreover, CL homopolymers with different functionality can also be separated from each other. The nature and amount of side products depends strongly on the catalyst used in the polymerization. The highest purity of the block copolymers is obtained with tin octoate.
