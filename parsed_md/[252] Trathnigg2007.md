## 1. Introduction 

In chromatography of polymers, retention is determined by the characteristic volumes of the column (pore volume _V_ p and interstitial volume _V_ i), the pore diameter _D_ , and the interaction parameter _c_ . While the influence of the pore diameter is predominant in size exclusion chromatography (SEC), the key parameters describing retention in liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) are the interaction parameter _c_ and the pore surface _S_ p of the column, which is related to the pore volume _V_ p and pore diameter _D_ . 

In SEC, _c_ is negative, in LAC it is positive. In liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC), _c_ = 0. If the parameters mentioned above are known with sufficient accuracy, one may predict the separation and even simulate chromatograms of very complex polymers [1–3]. The interaction parameter of a given repeat unit in LAC can be easily 

determined by a procedure published in recent papers [4,5]. The interaction parameter is independent on column dimensions and pore diameter and can thus be used as a measure of the interaction of a given repeat unit with the surface of a stationary phase in a given mobile phase. In LAC, the dependence of the interaction parameter on mobile phase composition is typically assumed to be linear, but there are also other dependencies discussed [6]. In a plot of interaction parameters versus mobile phase composition ϕ, different columns with the same chemical nature should fall quite well on one straight line, the intercept of which represents the interaction parameter in the poor solvent ( _c_ A), and the slope d _c_ /dϕ the strength of the good solvent. The intersection point of this line with the _x_ -axis corresponds to critical conditions. The retention of a given oligomer depends as well on the interaction parameter as on the pore dimensions (and thus the pore surface) of the stationary phase. As the interaction parameter of a given repeat unit in a given mobile phase is the same for stationary phases with the same chemical nature, retention can be adjusted by selection of the pore surface. This is especially important in the case of liquid chromatography under critical conditions, where retention of the invisible block cannot be adjusted by mobile phase composition or temperature [5,7,8]. 

The pore surface can be determined (in a similar way as the interaction parameter) from the elution volumes of non-functional oligomers [4,5,7]. Consequently, this approach is more relevant in LAC separations (which are run in mixed mobile phases) than the conventional method of determining the pore dimensions by inverse size exclusion chromatography (ISEC), which is typically performed in single mobile phase. 

If the parameters mentioned above ( _c_ A and d _c_ /dϕ) for a given system of repeat unit, stationary phase and mobile phase are known from a data base, and individual columns can be characterized easily with respect to their accessible volume and pore surface, the retention of polymers can be predicted and even simulated for highly complex architecture by a special software [2,3,12], which even allows a simulation of two-dimensional chromatography. The same approach can also be applied to gradient chromatography, as will be shown in another paper. 

In other words, such a data base would allow to select the starting conditions in method development much closer to the optimum, which can dramatically reduce time and solvent consumption required for the optimization of chromatographic conditions. ## 3. Results and discussion 

For prediction of chromatograms, the dependence of interaction parameters on mobile phase composition has to be known. Recently, Bashir and Radke [6] compared different models in LAC of PEG: the linear solvent strength model (LSSM), the quadratic solvent strength model (QSSM), both of which hold only in LAC, and the polymer model (PM), which can be applied in all modes of LC. 

In previous papers, we have described the determination of the important parameters for PEG on various alkyl-bonded stationary phases in methanol–water and acetone–water mobile phases [4,5,7]. In this study, we have now applied the same approach to other repeat units and mobile phases. The interaction parameters were determined from the slope in a plot according to Eq. (1), as this does not require the unambiguous identicifation of the individual oligomers (with respect to n). In general, the agreement of the results obtained from Eqs. (1), (3) and (4) is very good.

## 4. Conclusions 

The retention behaviour of polymers in LAC can be predicted very well using the accessible volume and pore surface of the column, which can be easily determined, and the interaction parameters from a data base. Such a data base can be established using data from isocratic LAC by a very simple procedure. 

Existing data can be used: the only requirement is the knowledge of the elution volumes of a sufficient number of consecutive oligomers with the same functionality. Elution volumes of the individual oligomers in an isocratic LAC separation can be predicted with reasonable accuracy using interaction parameters from a data base (which have been determined using other columns with the same chemical nature) and the characteristic parameters for the column used (accessible volume and pore surface), which could be determined in principle by one single measurement. In practice, three to five chromatograms will be satisfactory to obtain these parameters with good accuracy. In the range of interaction parameters typically used in LAC ( _c_ between 2.0 and 5.0) simple equations can be used to describe retention of homopolymers. The same approach can also be applied in gradient separations, as will be shown in another communication.
