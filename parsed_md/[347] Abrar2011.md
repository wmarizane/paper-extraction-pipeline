## **INTRODUCTION** 

Polymers are macromolecules composed of small structural units called “monomers” held together by covalent bonds. Since after the synthesis of very first synthetic polymer “Bakelite” in 1910, there is continuous development in the field of polymer synthesis. Beside the natural polymers, which are major constituent of life, synthetic polymers have become an important necessity of the modern day life. In fact, the research and developments in the polymer chemistry has contributed much in the development of modern day industry. Synthetic polymers include materials that are customarily called plastics and rubbers. These include such ordinary and inexpensive items as polyethylene grocery bags, nylon bags, and polyethylene glycol terephthalate soda bottles. On the other hand are much expensive and less common polymers having specialized properties including Kevlar[TM ] bullet proof vests, high temperature resistant fluorinated polymer seals in jet engines. We are surrounded by endless range of synthetic polymers ranging from high molecular mass polymeric materials to lower molar mass polymers and oligomers. 

Low molar mass polymers and oligomers have found broad range of applications in pharmaceutical, paints and dyestuff, medicines, agriculture, foods, personal care products and cosmetic industry  [1-3].  Polymer scientist are constantly introducing new grades of polymers and expanding the range of polymer products. 

Polymeric materials with special properties can be obtained by introduction of structural units different from the repeat unit (such as other monomer units or functional end groups). The purpose of these modifications can be different: functional end groups may allow further reactions of the polymer (such as chain extension etc.), copolymers consisting of more than one kind of repeat unit may combine the properties of the homopolymers to yield a product with completely new physical properties. 

The properties of a polymer depend, however, also on its architecture, i.e. the arrangement of the repeat units. Linear polymers are the simplest type of polymer architecture: they consist of a single backbone without branching. Branched polymers are composed of a

linear backbone with one, two or more branches emerging at different points from the main structure. Special types of branched polymers are star polymers [4,5], comb polymers [6], graft polymers [7,8], brush polymers [9]. In the case of copolymers, the arrangement of the individual segments or blocks has a very strong influence on the physical properties of the product. Block copolymers are often synthesized by living anionic polymerization and obtained as diblock, triblocks and multi block copolymers [10]. 

In high polymers, branching and chemical composition will strongly affect the product properties, while the functionality is not very important, as they may contain thousands of repeat unit per end group. In the range of lower molar mass, however, functionality, chemical composition and architecture are very important. 

Functional polymers may be used as such (for example as emulsifiers) [8,11,12] or as prepolymers to build up high molecular materials: hydroxy-terminated polymers can be used as precursors for polyurethanes [13-15](depending on their functionality, one may obtain elastomeric fibers, PU foam etc.), and polymers with polymerizable end groups can serve as macromonomers, from which graft copolymers or hydrogels can be synthesized [16]. 

If a polymer molecule contains blocks, which are not compatible with each other, this will lead to microphase separation. This effect is not observed with random copolymers. Block or graft copolymers, the blocks of which have a very different solubility, exhibit very special physical properties: they will be enriched on solid or liquid surfaces. These socalled amphiphilic polymers are surface-active, which is especially important, if they are composed of hydrophilic and hydrophobic (or lipophilic) segments. 

## **1.1.  Functional polymers** 

Functional polymers are often used as precursors for polymers with a special architecture [17]. This requires defined end groups, which can undergo condensation, addition or polymerization reactions. Telechelics are linear chains with two identical end groups. For example, polyethylene glycol (PEG) is a linear chain with two hydroxy end groups, which can be converted to ester or urethane groups. With diisocyanates, linear polyurethanes are formed, which can be used as fibers. Branched polyethers or polyesters will react with diisocyanates to form crosslinked polyurethanes [15,18]. 

The monomethyl ether of polyethylene glycol (PEG-MME) is a monofunctional molecule, the hydroxy group of which can react with (meth) acrylic acid or (meth) acryloyl chloride

## **1.2.  Amphiphilic polymers** 

Amphiphilic polymer comprises an important class of copolymers, which have gained increasing importance in the last decades [20,21]. The reason is their special structure, which impart them unusual properties. As amphiphilic polymers consist of hydrophobic and hydrophilic blocks, they are able to self-assemble in solution to form micelles  [22]. These polymers have found versatile applications as surfactants, drug delivery systems [2,23-25], pharmaceutical excipients [26], in personal care products [1,27], agrochemistry and in food products etc. Different types of polymers segments can be used as hydrophobic and hydrophilic block in amphiphilic copolymers depending on their properties [28]. Polyethylene glycol or PEG monomethy ether is widely used as hydrophilic block, while the Polypropylene oxide, Polycaprolactone, and Polylactide can be used a hydrophobic blocks [29]. Obviously, all kinds of architecture are - at least in principle - possible for such amphiphilic polymers. 

Block copolymers of Polyethylene oxide (PEO) and Polycaprolactone have been used as efficient drug delivery vehicles and for pharmaceutical and biomedical applications. Amphiphilic polymers may be used for surface modification of various materials [30,31]. When they are adsorbed on hydrophobic surfaces, they cover them with a hydrophilic layer, which may avoid coagulation [32]. One significant application of amphiphilic polymers is their use as surfactants that have advantageous properties as compared to tradition soups and other type of surface-active agents. In this class of polymers, the hydrophobic block is typically rather short (e.g. an alkyl group with typically 12 – 18 carbon atoms), and the hydrophilic block is often based on poly (ethylene oxide). 

## **1.3.  Polymeric Surfactants** 

Surfactants reduce the surface tension of a liquid, allowing its easy spreading and also lower the interfacial surface tension between two liquids, or between a liquid and a solid. These are the molecules that consist of a hydrophobic chain usually hydrocarbon or

fluorocarbon and a hydrophilic part that could be non-ionic, ionic or zwitterionic. They assemble in solutions into aggregates called micelles or vesicles [33]. The concentration of the surfactant at which micelles are formed is called critical micelle concentration (CMC). In water, tails of the micelles encapsulate oil droplet and their hydrophilic heads maintain the interaction with the water. However reverse micelles are formed when surfactant aggregates are formed in oil. Surfactants have found many applications in different chemical industries as detergents, paints, dyestuff, cosmetics, personal care products, pharmaceuticals, agrochemicals, fibers and plastics [3,34-36]. 

Surfactants are mainly classified on the basis of their hydrophilic part into four main groups, i.e. anionic, cationic, zwitterionic and non-ionic surfactants. Among these, an especially important class is polymeric surfactants [37]. Non-ionic surfactants are mostly based on ethylene oxide and alkyl or similar hydrocarbon groups. Ethoxylated surfactants are classified into various groups like fatty acid ethoxylates, fatty alcohol ethoxylates, alkyl phenol ethoxylates, ethylene oxide-propylene oxide copolymers, ethoxylated mono and diglycerides [38,39]. Polyhydroxy esters like sucrose esters, polyglycosides, glycerol ethoxylates, and sorbitan ester ethoxylates are well-known sugar based surfactants. These are based on the natural sources and produce non-toxic degradation products, which have made them more attractive. These are mostly used in food industries and have also found application in cosmetics and personal care products. Due to their mild dermatological properties and non-toxicity, sucrose esters have been used not only as food additives but also as excellent raw material for cosmetics, personal care and pharmaceutical products. Presence of a number of hydroxyl groups, made the esterification of sucrose rather complex and mainly a mixture of mono-, di- tri-, tetra- and penta esters is formed [40]. Since these products are very hydrophobic, their applications are limited. There is an esterification method developed to make the reaction selective, or some purification is performed to make the product rich in monoesters  [41]. Most of the methods are still limited because of their high cost and therefore the preparation by transesterifcation and then purification is still used for their synthesis [42]. Main producers for sucrose esters are Croda in USA, Dai-Ichi-Kogyo and Seiyaku in Japan and Goldschmidt in Germany. Their annual production is more or less 4,000 t/a, depending on the demand. Fatty alcohols and glucose react under acidic condition to form a complex mixture of mon, di, tri and oligoglycosides [43]. Major producers of alkyl glycosides are Henkel, Seppic, ICI, Kao, Union carbide and BASF with approximate production, 80,000t/a.

## **1.3.1.    Sorbitan esters and Polysorbates** 

Sorbitan esters are very important non-ionic surfactants. They were commercialized for the first time by Atlas Powder Company in 1938 under the trade name Span. These are produced by the esterification of sorbitol at high temperature (> 200 _C). Sorbitol dehydrates at this high temperature form isomers of which sorbitan and isosorbide are predominant (Scheme 1).  Esterification of these produce monoesters along with diesters and also some higher esters depending on the moles of fatty acids used. These surfactants are highly hydrophobic and have very low HLB value (1-8), making them insoluble in the polar solvents. Presence of higher esters like tri and tetra esters further increase their hydrophobicity, decreasing their efficiency in various applications. Recently, a two-step cascade reaction has been reported for the synthesis of sorbitan esters, producing higher content of monoesters [48]. To increase their hydrophilicity and further applications, these were ethoxylated. The ethoxylated products of sorbitan are called polysorbates. 

Polyoxyethylene sorbitan esters of fatty acids (also known as polysorbates) are well known non-ionic sugar based surfactants. These were commercialized in 1942 under trade name “Tween®” by Atlas Powder Company (now ICI America). Other trade names for polysorbate include, Monitan, Sorlate, and Olothorb. The name Tweens and polysorbates can be used interchangeably in literature. There are different types of polysorbates depending on the type of fatty acids that are used for their esterification.  The type of attached fatty acids is reflected by the name of polysorbate. They differ from each other in the length of the fatty acid chain, degree of ethoxylation and esterification. Mostly, fatty

The main causes of polydispersity in functionality and molar mass in polysorbates is: Fatty acids used for esterification have a mixed composition [58].  At high temperature of ethoxylation and esterification, a condensation of sorbitan hydroxy groups results in bicycle isosorbide, which contain two hydroxy groups for further ethoxylation and esterification. When spans are ethoxylated, the addition of ethylene oxide takes place only at the free hydroxy groups, while the ester bond should not react. At the applied high temperatures, transesterifcation reactions will also take place, raising complexity in the structure. 

As the ethoxylation proceeds at different rates on the four hydroxy groups, one must expect to obtain ethoxylated sorbitan with four arms of different lengths. Polyethylene glycol is also formed by chain transfer reactions in the presence of water in the reaction mixture [55,59]. Consequently, polysorbates are much more complex in composition and architecture than other non-ionic surfactants, and they show high polydispersity in their functionality and molar mass [48,55-57,60-62]. While fatty alcohol ethoxylates (FAEs), fatty acid methyl ester ethoxylates (FAMEEs) can be completely characterized using different modes of liquid chromatography [63-67], this is not very easy for the polysorbates. 

The emulsifying ability of surfactants depends on their chemical nature [26,49]. For polysorbates the activity of the composition is determined by the structure of the sorbitol derivative core (i.e. sorbitan and isosorbide), the alkyl chain length of fatty acids, the degree of esterification and ethoxylation over the sorbitan ring. Analysing the composition of polysorbates would help not only in predicting their properties but also in selecting their concentration to achieve the desired properties. In general, characterization of any polymeric heterogenous composition is the key step to determine the relationship between their properties, structure and composition. Polydispersity in polymer composition is due to distribution of their molar mass, chemical composition, functionality and architecture (linear, branched, star, graft copolymers). Therefore it is very important to characterize such complex polymeric surfactants and determine their actual composition.

## **1.4. Characterization of amphiphilic polymers** 

As most applications of polymers demand purity of their composition, characterization is very important to describe their activity. Homopolymers are distributed only in their molar mass (MMD).  In case of complex or heterogenous polymers there are a number of other distribution along with MMD; these are chemical composition distribution (CCD), Functionality type distribution (FTD) and distribution of architecture or morphology [68]. Of all the distributions of complex polymers, separation according to their architecture (topology) is still a challenge in the field of separation science. Although it’s difficult to characterize the branched polymer according to the distribution of their branches, the situation is worsen if the polymer composition is a heterogenous mixture and also contains their linear counterparts [69,70]. Therefore characterization of such complex polymers, which are distributed in more than one dimension, cannot be achieved using any analytical technique alone. For example any single separation technique determines the distribution in one dimension. Different techniques that have been used for the characterization of polymers and oligomers are liquid chromatography, NMR and MALDI TOF-MS [71-75]. In this thesis we have described a detailed study of characterization of complex compositions of commercial surfactants and some branched esters by employing different separation modes of high performance liquid chromatography. Different approaches of two-dimensional separation of polymers have been employed as offline 2-D HPLC, online 2-D HPLC and comprehensive two dimension separation. For precise determination and identifications of polymer composition, a hyphenated approach has been used combining HPLC and MALDI TOF-MS appropriately. This combination of mass spectrometric and liquid chromatography techniques has been proven to be a situation in which the limitation of each of them has been overcome. 

In the case of complex samples as polysorbates, important information can be obtained by degradation experiments: splitting off the fatty acids allows their identification and the independent analysis of the core.

## **1.5.  High performance liquid chromatography** 

There are various techniques available for the characterization of polymers of which high performance liquid chromatography has been widely used [78]. HPLC is an excellent analytical technique for the characterization of polymers and purification, identification and quantification of complex mixtures [79]. Due to a variety of available stationary phases, mobile phases, detectors and separation mechanisms, HPLC has revolutionized the characterization of complex polymers. There are different modes of separation in the liquid chromatography which differ from each other in their mechanism of separation [80]. In all these mechanisms separation is described by the interaction parameter c, which determine the interaction of the polymer chains with the stationary phase. A description of these modes of liquid chromatography has been given as follows. 

## **1.5.1.    Size exclusion chromatography** 

Size exclusion chromatography is a common analytical technique, which separate polymers according to their hydrodynamic volume in solution [81] i.e. entire molecular size. Ideal size exclusion chromatography is entirely driven by entropy (interaction 

12 

Chapter 1                                                                                                               Introduction 

parameter is negative c < 0) and there is no interaction of polymers with the surface of stationary phase i.e. enthalpy change is zero. Separation of polymers takes place in order of decreasing molar mass. Polymer chains larger than the largest pore of the stationary phase have no access to the stationary phase and elute at the interstitial volume. Smaller polymers have access to the pores of the stationary phase and elute at volumes somewhere between the interstitial volume and the void volume. 

Using appropriate calibration standards of known molar masses, the molar mass distribution of the unknown polymer chains can be determined [82]. The composition of the copolymer along the MMD can be determined by SEC with dual detection in a single solvent [83,84]. However SEC cannot distinguish between homopolymers and block copolymers. Moreover no information about the architecture and functionality type distribution is obtained. 

## **1.5.2.    Liquid Adsorption /Interaction chromatography** 

LAC is governed by enthalpy and separation is determined by the interaction of the polymer chains with the surface of the stationary phase. The interaction parameter is always positive in LAC (c > 1) and elution volume Ve of the polymer chains is always larger than the void volume Vo [85]. Polymer chains are eluted according to the strength of adsorption and the number of groups, which are capable to interact with the stationary phase. As the probability of being adsorbed and thus the retention of molecules increases exponentially with the number of adsorbing groups, samples with a broad MMD can often not be separated by isocratic elution: in a constant mobile phase composition, higher oligomers will elute very late, while the resolution of lower oligomers will not be sufficient. The separation of such samples can be done efficiently by varying the strength of mobile phase in the entire chromatogram. Gradient elution may allow a baseline separation of a quite large number of oligomers in reasonable time  [86,87]. This can be achieved by varying the composition of the mobile phase or the temperature [88]. Selection of the stationary phase and mobile phase determine the nature of the interaction of the polymer units with the stationary phase. 

In normal phase chromatography (NP-LAC) the stationary phase is more polar than the mobile phase, and molecules are separated according to the number and nature of polar groups.

In reversed phase chromatography (RP-LAC) a non-polar stationary phase in a comparatively polar mobile phase, consequently the separation is based on hydrophobic interaction. In RP-LAC molecules are separated according to the number of non-polar groups and the strength of their interaction with the stationary phase [89,90]. 

It must be mentioned, that PEGs can be separated by both techniques: the individual oligomers are separated on a RP column (e.g. C18 phases) in very polar mobile phases (methanol – water with less than 30 % methanol or acetone – water with less than 15 % acetone) [91] while a similar separation can be achieved on normal phase columns (plain silica) in acetone – water containing more than 80% acetone. 

A special case of normal phase chromatography is called hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC). The name was used for the first time by Andrew Alpert in 1990 [92]. In principle, any polar bound stationary phase can be used for separations in HILIC with strongly organic mobile phase [93]. The selection of mobile phase depends on the nature of the sample, however mostly acetonitrile-water, THF-water, dioxane are used; acetone-water can also be used for HILIC separations. Polar molecules are distributed between these two phases. ## **1.5.3.   Liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC)** 

Liquid chromatography at critical conditions is a separation mode in which entropy and enthalpic interactions of macromolecules compensate each other [96]. At this situation the change in Gibbs free energy of macromolecules is zero and the distribution coefficient is unity regardless of the length of their chains and all polymer chains of the same homologous series elute in one peak  [97,98]. Thus polymer chains that are chemically homogenous become chromatographically invisible and separation is directed by inhomogeneities in the polymer chain. Under such conditions, heterogeneities of the polymers such as end groups or other blocks (functionality) can be determined without any influence of the polymer repeat units. [99,100] 

On reversed phases, amphiphilic copolymers can be separated according to the length of the hydrophobic block [101]. Fatty ester ethoxylates or fatty alcohol ethoxylates (which are 

15 

Chapter 1                                                                                                               Introduction 

typically mixtures of different fatty acids) can be separated according to the length of hydrophobic fatty alkyl chain on non-polar stationary phase [67,93,102]. The transition of chromatographic separation from one mode to another mode can be achieved by changing the temperature or mobile phase composition.  For many polymers, critical condition can be achieved using binary mobile phases of a special composition, sometimes also in different combinations of solvents. For examples, critical conditions for PEG exist on typical reversed phase columns in aqueous mobile phases containing methanol, acetonitrile and acetone as organic modifier. In such mobile phases, quite different retention of other (non-polar) groups – such as alkyl groups or polyoxypropylene blocks - can be observed. In binary mobile phases, the retention of the hydrophobic block at the CAP for the hydrophilic chain may be insufficient or too high. This problem can be solved by using ternary mobile phases, where critical conditions exist along a critical adsorption line (CAL), which allows even gradient elution without loosing critical conditions [103,104]. 

## **1.6. Two dimensional liquid chromatography (2D-LC)** 

In the characterization of copolymers there may be different stages of complexity: while linear homopolymers have just a distribution of molar mass (MMD), which can be determined rather easily using simple methods, this is not the case for complex polymers, which may in addition have distributions of functionality and chemical composition, moreover they may differ in their architecture (linear, branched, stars, graft copolymers etc.). The determination of more than one distribution generally requires a twodimensional separation (and sometimes even multidimensional separations), in which at least one dimension will be liquid chromatography. Therefore development of 2D-LC has gained increasing attention in the last decade for the characterization of complex polymers [80,105] 

There are various advantages of the 2D-LC that offer the complete characterization of complex polymers along with some limitations [70]. The complete exploitation of the advantages of 2D-LC depends on the selection of the separation mechanisms in two dimensions that determine Orthogonality, peak capacity and sampling frequency [106,107]. 

One of the benefits of the 2D-LC is the increase in the peak capacity, which theoretically equals to the product of peak capacity of each dimension. However, practically the peak capacity in 2D-LC is reflected by orthogonality of the separation mechanisms. When the 

16 

Chapter 1                                                                                                               Introduction 

selected separation mechanisms in 2D-LC show completely different selectivities, then maximum values of peak capacity can be obtained and separation mechanisms are said to be orthogonal. The Orthogonality of separation is determined not only be separation mechanisms in two dimensions but also by the separation conditions and some properties of the sample. 

In principle, all modes of HPLC can be combined in a two dimensional separation: size exclusion chromatography (SEC), liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC), liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC), and liquid exclusion-adsorption chromatography (LEAC). These techniques can be combined off-line and on-line in twodimensional separations.  Depending on the way, sample is transferred from one dimension to the other dimension , 2D-LC can be either offline or online. 

## **1.6.1.    Offline Two dimension Separation of polymers** 

Offline 2D-LC is the most versatile 2D-LC separation. In the off-line approach fractions from the first dimension are typically collected using a fraction collector or manually [108,109].  If both mobile phases are compatible, the fractions can be injected directly into the second dimension. The fractions are just filled into autosampler vials; the second dimension can then be operated automatically under appropriate conditions. If the mobile phases used in both dimensions are not compatible with each other, the mobile phase from the first dimension is evaporated (e.g. by freeze drying) and subsequently dissolved in the mobile phase used in the second dimension. This procedure is laborious and requires careful handling of the fractions. It reconcentrates the sample components, but unfortunately also any impurity in the solvents. 

The direct injection of fractions is fast and simple, and it avoids the sources of error in sample handling, but suffers from the low sample concentration in some fractions. 

The off-line approach has one considerable advantage: fractions can be analyzed by different techniques, and it allows repeated analyses under optimized conditions. Moreover it is possible to analyze the fractions by spectroscopic techniques, among which MALDITOF-MS is the most promising one [110].

## **1.6.2.    On-line two-dimension separation (Comprehensive two dimension separation)** 

Online two dimension liquid chromatography (2D-LC) involves the transfer of the eluate from first dimension to the second dimension via an appropriate interface (typically a 6-, 8- , or 10-port switching valve).  The online coupling is possible if mobile phases in both dimensions are compatible to each other. The approaches used in the 2D-LC are Heartcutting (LC-LC) and comprehensive (LC × LC) chromatography: 

The eluate from entire chromatogram in the first dimension is collected in a loop of predefined volume, which is then subjected into second dimension. 

Some important factors that must be considered to determine the efficiency of comprehensive 2D-LC are the spreading angle, which is determined by non-Orthogonality of two dimensions, peak capacity ratio and retention correlation [105]. A two-dimensional separation is called comprehensive, if it meets three conditions [111]: Every part of the sample separated in first dimension should be subjected to analysis in the second dimension, equal percentage of all the sample components should be subjected to both dimension and detectors, separation that would be achieved in the first dimension should be maintained in the second dimension. 

## **1.6.3.    Problems in Comprehensive Two dimension separation** 

Although comprehensive two-dimensional chromatography offers enhanced separation efficiency, it suffers from some problems. The main problem in comprehensive chromatography is the mismatch of flow rates in the two dimensions and the sizes of the sample loops and the columns: as an entire chromatogram in the second dimension has to be finished in the time required to fill the loop in the first dimension. Consequently, the first dimension has to be run at a very low flow rate and the second one at a very high one, which may cause problems with the back pressure.  To solve the problem of high back pressure at the required high flow rates in the second dimension, one has to use short columns. In practice, monolithic columns offer the advantage of a very low backpressure. High flow rates are possible on such columns, but if an ELSD has to be used, this may cause detection problems. A reasonable sensitivity (which is highly important at the low concentrations in the second dimension) can be achieved at 2 ml/min. This means that with a loop volume of 200 µl the elution volume of a peak in the second dimension should not exceed 4 ml. The other problem is gradient elution for increasing sample resolution. While 

18 

Chapter 1                                                                                                               Introduction 

this is quite easy in the first dimension, gradient elution in the second dimension is problematic, as after each gradient there is not much time for re-establishing the original conditions.  Another serious problem is the sampling frequency: if the size of the sample loops is comparable to the peak width in the first dimension, two peaks will merge, even if they are separated to the baseline in the first dimension, and appear as a single peak in the second dimension. This effect is called undersampling. 

The selection of the separation mechanism in the second dimension is also an important concern in comprehensive two-dimensional chromatography. The separation in the second dimension should be fast enough to be finished in time. Therefore the separation mechanism in the second dimension may be SEC or LEAC (one block is in the SEC mode), which may compromise the separation efficiency. If we use adsorption condition (LAC) in the second dimension, then it may result in the wrap-over of the fraction to the next interval. 

Another important factor to be considered is the compatibility of the mobile phases used in the two dimensions, which does not necessarily mean the they are not miscible with each other. If the mobile phase used in the first dimension is a strong eluent in the second dimension, the injection of a rather large volume in the solvent stream of a weaker eluent in the second dimension may destroy the resolution in the second dimension. In this case there will be no retention and sample will elute with the solvent peak. 

After all, comprehensive chromatography is always a compromise, which may make sense in special cases, but requires very careful optimization. Quite often, other approaches may yield much better results. 

A comprehensive separation can also be performed off-line: If the relevant part of the chromatogram is continuously collected using a fraction collector, and the fractions thus obtained are transferred to the auto sampler of the second dimension, there is time enough in the second dimension to achieve a good resolution, and the first dimension can also be run at a reasonable flow rate. As has been shown previously [112], a full resolution of all oligomers in a diblock copolymer of ethylene oxide and caprolactone can be achieved in 5 – 6 hours. 

## **1.7.  MALDI TOF-MS for Polymer Characterization** 

Soft ionization techniques such as MALDI TOF MS and Electrospray ionization (ESI) have revolutionized the characterization of synthetic polymers since last decade. With

Complete information of polymer structures i.e. its molar mass, end group functionality and composition can be obtained by MALDI TOF-MS within very short analysis time [114]. 

MALDI TOF-MS is used to characterize polymers in three steps; Ionization of the intact polymer molecules in the gas phase, second step is the separation of gaseous lionized polymer species according to their mass to charge ratio and then detection of the separated ion. In the ionization step of MALDI TOF MS, polymer molecules, matrix and salt of an organic compound are deposited on the target plate together. Then laser is fired on the target plate, and the ultraviolet absorbing matrix absorbs the laser light and transfers it efficiently to the polymer molecules, thus protecting the polymers from excessive amount of energy. As a result of this laser excitation, radical cations, anions or excited species are formed by the removal or addition of electrons from the polymer or by addition of other charged species. Synthetic polymers cannot be easily charged; therefore addition of some organic salt to the matrix polymer mixture is required. MALDI forms only single charged polymer species irrespective of the charged state of the metal [115,116]. 

In the next step after ionization, polymer molecules are separated according to their charge to mass ratio (m/z).  Time of flight mass analyzer is most commonly used in the MALDI for the separation of ions. A high voltage accelerates the ions, which enter into an evacuated tube called drift tube. In the drift tube ions are separated according to their velocity. 

There are two modes of operation for the TOF analyzer; linear mode and reflector mode. The main difference between the two modes is that in the reflector mode, ions are reflected by an electrical mirror. This enhances the separation of ions due to longer flight time available to the ion travelling. Finally ions reach the ion detector; most commonly multi channel plate (MCP) detector is used. Ions striking the detector produce the secondary electron, which amplifies each ion impact signal. Then secondary electrons are measured by photo multiplication [117].

## **2.1. Abstract** 

Polyoxyethylenes with different functionality and architecture can be separated according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups on polar stationary phases in acetone water mobile phases containing 90 –97 % acetone. The best results were obtained on a HILIC column. Typical samples, which can be analyzed by this technique, are polyethylene glycols, their mono- and dialkyl ethers, macromonomers, the fatty esters of PEG and ethoxylated glycerol.

## **2.2. Introduction** 

Polyoxyethylenes are hydrophilic polyethers, which have many applications in industrial manufacturing, medicines and personal care products [1-3]. Polyoxyethylenes with terminal hydroxy groups and a molar mass below 20,000 g/mol are typically referred to as polyethylene glycols (PEG), samples with higher molar mass as polyethylene oxides (PEO). Polyoxyethylenes of different molar masses may have different physical properties but same chemical properties. 

PEGs and their derivatives are synthesised by anionic polymerisation of ethylene oxide with various catalysts and initiators, resulting in products with different functionality. There are also stars with varying number of arms available. 

Difunctional PEG (with two hydroxy groups) are obtained with water or a diol (e.g. di- or triethylene glycol). Monofunctional PEGs (with only one terminal hydroxy group) are synthesized using a monofunctional alcohol as initiator [4,5]. Diethers of PEG [6] are obtained by deactivation of the growing chain by suitable coupling reactions. PEG derivatives with higher functionality can be obtained using multivalent alcohols (such as glycerol, trimethylol-propane, pentaerithrol etc.). Other functionalities can be introduced by derivatization of the terminal hydroxy groups. 

Polyoxyethylene is also used as hydrophilic block in the synthesis of amphiphilic surfactants that have applications in the pharmaceutical, cosmetic and food industries. Fatty alcohol ethoxylates can only be synthesized by ethoxylation of fatty alcohols, while the fatty esters of PEG derivatives can be obtained by two different routes: ethoxylation of fatty acids, fatty esters, mono, di- and triglycerides or esterification of the hydroxy groups of PEG or other ethoxylates with fatty acids. Obviously, both routes will result in a mixture of the desired product (e.g. a monoester of PEG) with the unreacted starting materials and side products (fatty acids, PEG, and diesters). 

Ethoxylated glycerides are well known safe non-ionic surfactants [7-9] that have been used in the food processing, textile applications, and leather processing, pharmaceutical, cosmetic and as agriculture ingredients. In cosmetics they reduce the irritation potential of some aggressive surfactants. Other widely used ethoxylates of natural sugars are Sorbitol ethoxylates and its esters known as Polysorbates or Tweens [10,11]. 

PEGs are also used for the synthesis of polyurethanes [12]: polymer segments derived from PEG impart flexibility to the polyurethane for their applications as elastomeric fibers. 

31 

Chapter 2                                                                                       Characterization by HILIC 

Another application is the synthesis of macromonomers [13-15] and crosslinkers for hydrogels: Monoacrylates or methacrylates of PEG can be polymerized (or copolymerized with other monomers) to yield graft copolymers with a (meth)acrylate backbone and PEG grafts.  Dimethacrylates are crosslinkers for the production of hydrogels, which can be used as solid electrolytes [16-18]. In the synthesis of soluble graft copolymers, a macromonomers must not contain dimethacrylates, as this would lead to crosslinked products. Obviously, a full characterization of functional PEGs is crucial for many applications. This requires the determination of their molar mass distribution (MMD) and functionality type distribution (FTD). A rather difficult task is also the quantitative determination of other functionalities in trace amounts. As has been shown previously, this can be done by combinations of different chromatographic techniques (which will be discussed later on) and MALDI-TOF-MS [1,19-24]. The latter is an excellent tool in the identification of different functionalities, it is, however, rather problematic in the quantification of trace amounts, especially such of low molar mass. 

In this paper, we have tried to find new approaches in HPLC, which would allow a separation of polyoxyethylenes with different functionalities under conditions, which also allow a reliable quantitative determination of impurities even in trace amounts.

## **2.4. Results and discussion** 

Functional polymers and oligomers can be analyzed by HPLC in different modes, which are characterized by the so-called interaction parameter [25]. In size exclusion (SEC), separation is driven by the entropy change, and the interaction parameter is strongly negative. Polymers are separated according to their size. Large molecules, which have no access to the pores of the stationary phase, are eluted earlier, while smaller ones spend more time in the pores and are eluted later. SEC is in widespread use for the determination of the molar mass distribution (MMD) of polymers [1]. No information is, however, obtained on functionality. Liquid Adsorption chromatography (LAC) is typically governed by the enthalpy change, and the interaction parameter is positive. Retention increases with the number of groups, which may interact with the stationary phase. In Liquid Chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) enthalpic and entropic effects compensate each other, and retention becomes independent on molar mass. 

## **2.4.1.  Liquid Adsorption chromatography (LAC)** 

In LAC, the separation order depends on the nature of the stationary and mobile phase. In normal phase systems (where the stationary phase is more polar than the mobile phase), retention increases with the number of polar groups. If the end groups can interact with the stationary phase, their interaction parameter (the end group parameter _q_ [26,27]) must also be taken into account. 

In the case of PEG derivatives, this means, that retention depends on these parameters, on the number of EO units and the number of hydroxy groups. As is shown in Figure 1, the individual oligomers of PEG derivatives can be resolved on a silica column in 90 -95 % acetone, and retention increases in the order PEG-DME < PEG-MME < PEG. In reversed phase systems, retention depends on the number of EO units and alkyl end groups [26,27]. As has been shown previously, a full resolution of all oligomers in a mixture of PEG and PEG-MME can be achieved under appropriate conditions  [28]. At a special mobile phase composition and temperature the second homologous series can be shifted in a way, that all peaks elute exactly between those of the main series. This is, however, not possible for

more complex samples, which contain more than two polymer homologous series. In this case, the influence of the main chain on retention has to be suppressed.

## **2.4.2.  Liquid chromatography at critical conditions** 

In a special mobile phase composition (and temperature), the polymer chain becomes “chromatographically invisible” [29-32]. This is called the critical adsorption point (CAP), where entropic and enthalpic contributions compensate each other, and the interaction parameter equals zero. At the CAP for the EO unit, all polyethylene oxide chains elute at same elution volume regardless of their length. This separation mode allows a separation according to the end groups or other blocks. For functional polymers and block copolymers, the interaction parameter of the other block (or the end group parameter) determines the elution behaviour. If the polymer contains another block, the interaction parameter of which is negative, the copolymer elutes in SEC mode, i.e. earlier than the homopolymer at the CAP, which allows a determination of the MMD of this block. 

If a polymer contains one end group (or block), which is adsorbed at the CAP of the repeat unit, all oligomers elute in a sharp peak (independent on molar mass) at an elution volume

corresponding to that of the corresponding end group, and are separated from the nonfunctional oligomers, which also elute as a narrow peak at the void volume of the column [33,34]. Under such conditions, polymer homologous series with different functionality can be separated from each other. Monofunctional molecules and A-B diblocks (with an adsorbing block A) elute at the CAP for B as narrow peaks, and so do B- A-B triblocks. 

On a reversed phase column, this can even be achieved, if the end groups are not very different from the repeat unit, as is the case with PEG and PEG-MME: under critical conditions for PEG on a reversed phase column in acetonitrile – water, acetone – water or methanol-water mobile phases both series elute as narrow peaks, which are well separated from each other  [4,19,20]. 

A very different elution behaviour is, however, observed with A-B-A triblocks (or difunctional chains) [30,35]. ## **2.5. Conclusions** 

The results described above suggest a somewhat different mechanism compared to the typical one in LCCC: if the separation would be based on adsorption, difunctionals should be resolved as predicted by the theory [30,35]. Evidently, the chromatograms obtained in this study do not show this behaviour, which leads to the assumption, that the separation on these columns should rather be based on partitioning into the aqueous (or at least waterrich) layer at the surface of the stationary phase. Further studies shall show the thermodynamic background of these separations.

## **3.1. Abstract** 

Polyoxyethylene macromonomers are analyzed by one-dimensional liquid chromatography under different conditions, depending on the required information. These samples may contain polyethylene glycol (PEG) and the corresponding di(meth)acrylate besides the desired mono(meth)acrylate. The molar mass distribution (MMD) of the PEG and the monoester can be obtained by liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) on a reversed phase column in acetone – water with a gradient from 10 to 20 % acetone. The MMD of the diesters can be obtained with isocratic elution by liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) on a reversed phase column in 31 % acetone, or using size exclusion (SEC) conditions for PEG and LAC conditions for the end groups, which is the case in 40 – 55 % acetone. The absolute amount of the series with different functionality can be obtained by LCCC in ternary mobile phases consisting of acetone, methanol, and water along the critical adsorption line (CAL). Under such conditions, all series elute as narrow peaks (regardless their MMD), which can easily be integrated and quantified.

## **3.2. Introduction** 

Amphiphilic graft copolymers with a hydrophopic backbone and hydrophilic grafts can be synthesized by (co)polymerization of macromonomers[1-3]: Monoacrylates methacrylates of polyethylene glycol (PEG) or its monoalkyl or -aryl ethers can be polymerized (or copolymerized with other monomers) to yield graft copolymers with a (meth)acrylate backbone and PEG grafts [4-8]. The corresponding di(meth)acrylates are crosslinkers for the production of hydrogels, which can be used as solid electrolytes[9-11]. Macromonomers are typically produced by reaction of PEG or PEG monomethyl ether (PEG-MME) with (meth)acrylic acid or (meth)acryloyl chloride. 

In the following text, the abbreviations PEG-MA (PEG monomethacrylate), ME-PEG-MA (methoxy-PEG monomethacrylate) and PEG-DMA (PEG dimethacrylate) will be used for the mono- and difunctional chains, respectively. 

In the case of PEG (with two terminal hydroxy groups) this will not yield the desired pure monoester: the product will always also contain unreacted PEG and the diester (Scheme 1). The residual PEG is just useless, but does not seriously affect the properties of the product. In the synthesis of soluble graft copolymers, however, a macromonomer must not contain dimethacrylates, as this would lead to crosslinked products. 

Diesters may also be present in ME-PEG-MA: as has been discussed previously[12-14], PEG-MME always contains PEG (which results from chain transfer to water during the polymerization), and therefrom mono- and diesters may be formed. It must be mentioned, that the PEG in PEG-MME has typically a higher molar mass, as it can grow in both directions and therefore its average degree of polymerization can be approximately twice as large. 

Consequently, a full characterization of the macromonomers is crucial for many applications. Such a characterization requires the independent determination of the molar mass distribution (MMD) and the functionality type distribution (FTD). This can be done by combinations of different chromatographic techniques and matrix-assisted laser desorption time-of-flight mass spectroscopy (MALDI-TOF-MS)[12, 15-19]. The latter is an excellent tool in the identification of different functionalities, it is, however, rather problematic in the quantification of small amounts (relative concentrations), especially such of low molar mass. 

50

While the MMD of the different polymer homologous series should be the same in PEGMA (as the diesters are formed from the same PEG as starting material), the MMD of mono- and diesters may be different in ME-PEG-MA, as has been mentioned above. 

An especially difficult task is the quantitative determination of other functionalities, if they are present in trace amounts. This is, however, important in the analysis of macromonomers, as the presence of only a few per cent of di(meth)acrylates will result in the formation of hydrogels instead of the desired soluble graft copolymers. For the crosslinking reaction the molar concentration of the diester is relevant, hence it is necessary to determine the amount and the average molar mass of the diester fraction. 

A separation according to molar mass can be achieved by different modes of liquid chromatography, as will be discussed later on. For the main components, this is typically quite easy, while it may be a serious problem for the minor components. 

Very small amounts can only be reasonably detected and quantified, if they elute as a narrow peak (regardless the length of the polymer chain). On reversed phase columns, such a separation of non-functionals and monofunctionals can easily be achieved, while difunctionals show a different peak pattern[20-23]: they are separated according to the length of the chain, which means, that small amounts of diesters will fall below the detection limit. 

In another paper[24], we have described the separation of PEG and the corresponding mono- and dimethacrylates according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups. This can be achieved on a HILIC column in 95 to 97 % acetone – water. Under such conditions the different homologous series elute as well-resolved narrow peaks, which can be easily integrated. Obviously, no discrimination of macromonomers without a terminal hydroxy group (such as methoxy-PEG monomethacrylate) and the dimethacrylates is possible by this approach. A discrimination of the individual series according to alkyl or acyl end groups can only be achieved on reversed phases. We have thus tried to find other systems, which would allow such a separation.

## **3.6. Conclusions** 

In the analysis of macromonomers different analytical problems may arise, which can be solved by different approaches. The first question concerns the molar mass distributions of the individual polymer homologous series, which may be present in such a sample. The non-functional PEG and the monofunctional series (PEG-MME and PEG-MA) can be analyzed by LAC with isocratic or gradient elution. The monofunctional PEG-MA might in principle also be analyzed by LEAC, but as the interaction of the end group on a reversed phase column is not strong enough, this is not the method of choice. The difunctionals ME-PEG-MA and PEG-DMA can be separated according to the number of repeat units at the CAP for the EO unit, but also in the SEC range for PEG. It does not make sense to run a gradient from LAC to SEC, as in LAC range the elution volumes of the individual oligomers of the non- and monofunctionals increase with the number _n_ of repeat units, while those of the difunctionals decrease with increasing _n_ . For the quantitative determination of small amounts of difunctionals, this separation must be suppressed, which can be achieved with gradient elution in ternary mobile phases along the critical adsorption line.

## **4.2. Introduction** 

Polysorbates (often also called by their commercial name Tween) are amphiphilic molecules, which can be used in a variety of cosmetics, food and pharmaceutical products as emulsifier, stabilizer and dispersant. They are fatty esters of polyoxyethylated sorbitan, which are synthesized by subsequent ethoxylation and esterification of sorbitol. The major food groups contributing to the dietary intake of polysorbates are confectionery, ices, deserts, fine bakery wares, milk analogues, emulsifies sauces, chewing gums, fat emulsions for baking. An important advantage is their low toxicity. The acceptable daily intake (ADI) for polysorbates is 10mg/kg body weight [1]. Another application of increasing importance is the formulation of biotherapeutics [2,3]. Polysorbates are added to protein solutions against physical damage during processing and storage. They are biocompatible stabilizers, which efficiently prevent surface adsorption of the protein.  Polysorbates are also used to produce nanostructured lipid carriers for drug delivery systems [4]. 

There are two possible routes for the synthesis of Tweens  [1,5-12] 

1. Sorbitol is esterified with fatty acids or anhydrides, which is typically performed with acid catalysis at 130 – 180°C (Scheme 1). At the temperature required for the esterification water is eliminated from sorbitol to form three possible isomers of sorbitan (Scheme 2) and (with elimination of another water molecule) isosorbide. To increase their hydrophilicity, these are ethoxylated to produce Tweens (Scheme 4). 

2. Sorbitol is reacted with typically 20 moles of ethylene oxide molecules, which is typically performed with basic catalysis at 200 – 250°C. Under the reaction conditions of the ethoxylation sorbitol is converted to sorbitan (three different isomers, see (Scheme 1) and isosorbide (Scheme 2). Mass spectrometry can, however, not discriminate oligomers having same molecular weight but different architecture. A full characterization of polysorbates requires determination of molar mass distribution (MMD) and functionality type distribution (FTD). In addition, information on the architecture should be obtained. Basically various chromatographic techniques which differ in their separation mechanism can be applied for the analysis of polysorbates e.g. thin layer chromatography [21], supercritical fluid chromatography (SFC) [14], and high performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) [2,3,12,21,22]. Mono, di, tri and tetra esters of sorbitan could be separated using HPLC [13] or SFC [14].  In the separation of polymers and oligomers, liquid chromatography is most frequently used technique. Different chromatographic modes can be applied varying in their separation mechanism and detection. These techniques can be used in combination to achieve multidimensional separation. 

- Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) 

It separates the components according to their molecular size, hence it yields information about the molar mass distribution (MMD). By using dual detection one may also determine the chemical composition of the sample along the MMD [29-31]. SEC is, however, not capable to give information about the functionality of the molecules. 

- Liquid Adsorption chromatography (LAC) 

It separates the component of a mixture according to the number of structural units, which interact with the stationary phase (in polymers this is typically the repeat unit). LAC often allows a baseline separation of oligomers, but due to strong adsorption of higher oligomers, these cannot be separated under isocratic conditions, therefore gradient elution has to be used. In this case the Evaporating Light Scattering detector (ELSD) must be applied. 

- Liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC) 

LCCC, which is often also called liquid chromatography at critical adsorption point (CAP) [16,32-35], is run at special temperature and mobile phase composition. At the CAP all chains with the same repeat unit elute at the same elution volume (regardless of their length), that’s mean polymer chains become chromatographically invisible. In this case separation according to structure units other than repeating unit, e.g. with respect to end

group etc can be achieved. Non-functional homopolymers elute at the void volume of the column. LCCC is run under isocratic conditions but typically in mixed mobile phases.

## **4.2.1. Quantitation in SEC of copolymers and functional polymers** 

In SEC of polymers consisting of two different structural units A and B the sensitivity of the detector for the components A and B may be very different. In this case, one will obtain erroneous results using just one detector. 

Using a combination of two concentration detectors (such as RI, UV and density detector [40]), which have different response factors for the units A and B one may obtain information on the chemical composition and thus an accurate result. 

As the polymers to be analyzed in this study do not have any UV-absorbing groups, the combination of density and RI detection is the method of choice. With amphiphilic copolymers, this works, however, only in SEC, where single mobile phases are used. In mixed mobile phases the polymer molecules are preferentially solvated by one component of the mobile phase, hence the response of the detector will depend on the amounts of the components A an B as well as that of the solvent travelling with the peak. This would require a third concentration detector, which is not available. The evaporative light scattering detector (ELSD) cannot be applied for this purpose because of its unclear response to copolymers. 

If the individual oligomers can be sufficiently separated and their response factors can be determined (or calculated), dual detection can be used to study preferential solvation [41].

## **4.3.  Experimental part** 

These investigations were performed using a density detector (according to the mechanical oscillator principle) DDS70 (CHROMTECH, Graz, Austria) in all chromatographic systems. Data acquisition and processing was performed using the software CHROMA, which has been developed for the DDS70. 

In SEC, we used a modular system consisting of a Gynkotek 300C pump, a VICI injector equipped with a 100 µl sample loop, two column selection valves Rheodyne 7060 (Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA), a density detection system DDS 70 (Chromtech, Graz, Austria) coupled with an ERC 7512 RI detector (ERMA, Japan). 

SEC measurements were performed on PLgel 100Å column (7.8 × 600 mm, 5 µm, from Polymer Laboratories, Church Stretton, Shropshire, UK) at a flow-rate of 1.00 ml/min. Sample concentrations were 3.0 - 10.0 g/l. The columns were calibrated with PPGs from Sigma-Aldrich and Fluka (Buchs, Switzerland). Molar mass distributions were calculated using CHROMA. 

LCCC was performed on an analytical and on a semipreparative scale. 

In the analytical system, a Waters 2414 refractive index detector (Waters, Milford, MA, USA) was connected to the DDS 70. The mobile phase was delivered by a JASCO 880 PU pump (from Japan Spectrosopic Company, Tokyo, Japan) at a flow rate of 0.5 ml.min[-1] . Samples were injected manually using a Rheodyne 7125 injection valve (from Rheodyne, Cotati, CA, USA) equipped with an 50 µl loop. Figure 2 shows a chromatogram of Tween 65, which was obtained by SEC with dual detection (on PLgel 100Å in chloroform). As all peaks in such a solvent with high density are negative, the density trace is shown here with reversed sign. This sample has a trimodal MMD, and it contains two low molecular components, which elute at about 15 ml. Obviously, the density and the RI trace look different: The second maximum in the main peak is higher in the density trace, where the low molecular peaks have the same sign, while this is not the case in the RI trace. One may speculate, that the low molecular peaks originate from the free fatty acids. This is indeed the case, as can be seen in Figure **3** : Tween 65 contains considerable amounts of fatty acids, while Tween 60 contains only traces of free acids. Moreover, the MMD of Tween 60 is unimodal, while Tween 65 has a trimodal distribution. This could be due to the side products mentioned in the introduction. When such a sample is analyzed by SEC with dual detection, the chemical composition along the MMD can be obtained, if the response factors of the components and their molar mass dependence are known for both detectors. Unfortunately, the hydrophilic part is not very well defined; hence we decided to choose PEG as a model, and to neglect the sorbitan of isosorbide unit (which should not make too much effect, as the number of EO units is typically high enough). As a model for the hydrophobic unit we have chosen fatty acid methyl esters (FAME). This is as well an approximation, as typically technical fatty acids are used in the production of polysorbates. 

As can be seen in (Figure 4), the response factors of PEG and fatty esters are negative in density detection. The density detector is much more sensitive for the fatty esters than for PEG, and the molar mass dependence of response factors is not very pronounced in both cases. The RI detector is very sensitive for PEG (with a positive response factor), while it does not detect the C16 and C18 fatty esters. For the esters with less carbon atoms the response factors are negative.  Using the response factors thus obtained, we have determined the chemical composition along the MMD by SEC with density and RI detection (Figure 5). Evidently, the low molecular fraction of Tween 61 contains less fatty acid, which is reasonable, if this fraction consists of the monoesters (and maybe also of PEG and ethoxylated sorbitan or isosorbide). The higher molecular fractions should contain di-, tri- and maybe tetraesters, which agree with the increase of the fatty acid content with molar mass.

## **4.5. Conclusions** 

Different modes of liquid chromatography can be used to separate the complex mixture of polysorbates. Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) and liquid chromatography under critical conditions were used in this study to determine the molar mass distribution and chemical composition of different polysorbates. Using SEC with dual detection, the chemical composition along the molecular mass was determined. LCCC answer the question of functionality of these polysorbates. Reversed phase columns with different mobile phases can be separated according to functionality. Microwave-assisted methanolysis allows the determination of the fatty acids and the size of the hydrophilic core. 

Tween 20 contains monoester of lauric, mystiric and palmitic acid, Tween 40 and 60 contain mono and diesters of palmitic and stearic acid. Tween 65, which is specified as triester, contains also mono and diesters as well as free fatty acids. 

The next step will be the fractionation of polysorbates according to functionality and the subsequent analysis using other modes of chromatography and other analytic techniques, such as mass spectrometry.

## **Characterization of complex polymers by two dimensional liquid chromatography** 

## **5.1. Abstract** 

Complex polymers were characterized by combinations of different chromatographic separation mechanisms: liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC), liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC), and liquid exclusion-adsorption chromatography (LEAC). These techniques were combined off-line and on-line in two-dimensional separations. Fatty acid ethoxylates, fatty esters of polyethylene glycol (PEG) and polysorbates were analyzed by two-dimensional liquid chromatography with liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) as the first and liquid exclusion adsorption chromatography (LEAC) as the second dimension. A full separation of all oligomers to the baseline could be achieved in both dimensions. In two-dimensional separations, the offline approach is compared to comprehensive chromatography, and the scope and limitations of both techniques are discussed

## **5.2. Introduction** 

In the characterization of polymers there may be different stages of complexity: while linear homopolymers have just a distribution of molar mass (MMD), which can be determined rather easily using simple methods, this is not the case for complex polymers, which may in addition have distributions of functionality and chemical composition, moreover they may differ in their architecture (linear, branched, stars, graft copolymers etc.). The determination of more than one distribution generally requires a twodimensional separation (and sometimes even multidimensional separations), in which at least one dimension will be liquid chromatography. 

A highly promising technique is matrix-assisted laser-desoption ionization time-of-flight mass spectroscopy (MALDI-TOF-MS). When the first of these instruments appeared on the market, many researchers expected, that this new technique could solve all problems and would sooner or later make chromatography obsolete. Despite its high potential, it is problematic in the analysis of complex samples, when raw materials are analyzed. In some kinds of samples the same molar mass may be related to several molecules with different compositions and architecture. Moreover, mass spectroscopy does not yield any information on architecture (such as a discrimination of di- and triblocks). The results obtained with fractions from chromatography are, however, much easier to interprete and typically unambiguous. 

Polymers can be separated by liquid chromatography in different modes: Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) separates according to molecular size: elution volumes decrease with increasing molar mass. Liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) separates according to the number of groups capable of being adsorbed on the surface of the stationary phase, consequently, retention increases exponentionally with the number of adsorbing groups in a molecule. A characteristic parameter for these mechanisms is the so-called interaction parameter _c_ [1]. In SEC, c is negative, in LAC it is positive. Between these extremes there is a situation, where _c_ = 0. At a special mobile phase composition and temperature – the so-called critical adsorption point (CAP) – the polymer chain becomes “chromatographic invisible”  [2-5], which means, that all chains (regardless their molar mass) elute at the same volume: in the case of non-functional chains at the void volume, while monofunctional chains with adsorbing end groups elute later, but still a narrow peaks. This technique is called liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC). A diblock copolymer with one block (A) at the CAP ( _cA_ = 0) and the other one (B) eluting in the SEC 

99 

Chapter 5                                                                                  Characterization by 2D HPLC 

regime ( _cB_ < 0) will elute earlier, i. e. in SEC mode, which allows the determination of the MMD of block B. 

At the CAP for A ( _cA_ = 0), A-B diblocks and A-B-A triblocks show the same elution behaviour. A different situation is, however, observed with difunctional chains B-A-B at the CAP for A and adsorbing conditions for the end groups. In this case, retention depends on the size of the critical block! The oligomers elute in SEC order: all polymer chains will elute earlier than a molecule consisting of just two end groups and no repeat units. Obviously, the same situation will also be observed with B-A-B triblocks, where _cA_ = 0 and _cB_ > 0. 

Another situation can also be utilized: If in a diblock A-B the individual interaction parameters have the opposite sign ( _cA_ > 0 and _cB_ < 0 or vice versa), the individual oligomers elute in SEC order, but far behind the void volume. This technique is called Liquid exclusion adsorption chromatography (LEAC)  [6]. It allows a baseline separation of the lower oligomers (up to n = 20) of non-ionic surfactants under isocratic conditions. 

Using a special software [7], the chromatographic behaviour of even complex polymers may be predicted on the basis of the interaction parameter and several other parameters describing the column (interstitial volume, pore volume, pore diameter). These parameters can be easily determined, as has been described previously [8,9]. These mechanisms can be combined in two-dimensional separations, in which the transfer of fractions from the first to the second dimension can be performed off-line or on-line. 

## **5.2.1.    Approaches in two-dimensional chromatography** 

In the off-line approach fractions from the first dimension are typically collected using a fraction collector. If the mobile phases used in both dimensions are not compatible with each other, the mobile phase from the first dimension is evaporated (e.g. by freeze drying) and subsequently dissolved in the mobile phase used in the second dimension. This procedure is laborious and requires careful handling of the fractions. It reconcentrates the sample components, but unfortunately also any impurity in the solvents. If both mobile phases are compatible, the fractions can be injected directly into the second dimension. The fractions are just filled into autosampler vials, the second dimension can be operated automatically under appropriate conditions [10]. This procedure is fast and simple, and it avoids the sources of error in sample handling, but suffers from the low sample concentration in some fractions. 

100 

Chapter 5                                                                                  Characterization by 2D HPLC 

The off-line approach has one considerable advantage: fractions can be analyzed using different techniques, and it allows repeated analyses under optimized conditions. Moreover it is possible to analyze the fractions by spectroscopic techniques, among which MALDITOF-MS is the most promising one [11]. 

The on-line approach does not involve any handling of fractions: the eluate from the first dimension is transferred to the second dimension via an appropriate interface (typically a 6-, 8-, or 10-port switching valve) [12]. Obviously, on-line coupling can only be applied, if the mobile phases in both dimensions are compatible. Basically, there are two different approaches: Heart-cutting and comprehensive chromatography. In heart-cutting, single fractions from the first dimension are directly injected into the second dimension. Alternatively, the fractions may be stored in a short column, where they are completely adsorbed. When the storage column is switched into the second dimension, which runs in a stronger eluent, the fraction is focused and reconcentrated. Of course, a new injection is required for each peak in the first dimension. This works well, if a sufficient resolution can be achieved in the first dimension. 

In comprehensive chromatography, the eluate from an entire chromatogram in the first dimension is collected in aliquots of predefined volumes, which are subsequently injected into the second dimension in an automated way. Obviously, before a fraction from the first dimension is injected in the second dimension, the previous one must be completed analyzed. 

## **5.2.2.    Problems in comprehensive chromatography** 

The main problem in comprehensive chromatography is the mismatch of flow rates and the sizes of the sample loops and the columns: as an entire chromatogram in the second dimension has to be finished in the time required to fill the sample loop in the first dimension, the first dimension has to be run at a very low flow rate (far from the van Deemter optimum), and the second one at a very high one (which may cause problems with the back pressure). This may be illustrated by the following example: In our case, the Ultimate 3000 pump allows flow rates between 0.1 and 10 ml/min. Hence the flow rate in the first dimension was set to 0.1 ml/min. Under such conditions, a chromatogram on a typical analytical column will take 2 – 3 hours. Using sample loops with 100 or 200 µl, the time to fill the loop equals 1 or 2 min, which is the available time to complete a separation in the second dimension. This means, that one has to use short columns and high flow 

101 

Chapter 5                                                                                  Characterization by 2D HPLC 

rates. In practice, monolithic columns should be preferred because of their low back pressure. On such a column, high flow rates are possible, but if an ELSD has to be used, this will cause detection problems. A reasonable sensitivity (which is highly important at the low concentrations in the second dimension) can be achieved a 2 ml/min. With a loop volume of 200 µl, this means, that the elution volume of a peak in the second dimension should not exceed 4 ml. If one considers, that the void volume of a column with the dimensions 100x4.6 mm may equal about 1 ml, there is not much space left for a good resolution. On the other hand, injecting 200 µl on such a small column is also quite much, if not too much. With 100 µl loop, however, there is just half the time available for a separation, hence the flow rate in the second dimension must be much higher. This will waste a lot of solvents: a chromatogram of 3 hours will consume 1.2 l of mobile phase, if the flow rate in the second dimension is 4 ml/min. The next question concerns the mechanism in the second dimension, in which a separation is definitely finished after a given time. This may be SEC or LCCC with the second block in SEC mode, both of which have a rather poor efficiency. In LAC there may be a wrap-over of a fraction to the next interval. 

An often neglected fact is also the influence of the mobile phase of the first dimension on retention in the second dimension: if such a large volume of a strong eluent is injected in a solvent stream of a weaker eluent, there may be no retention at all, and the sample will just elute with the solvent peak. 

After all, comprehensive chromatography is always a compromise, which may make sense in special cases, but requires very careful optimization. Quite often, other approaches may yield much better results. 

A comprehensive separation can also be performed off-line: If the relevant part of the chromatogram is continuously collected using a fraction collector, and the fractions thus obtained are transferred to the autosampler of the second dimension, there is time enough in the second dimension to achieve a good resolution, and the first dimension can also be run at a reasonable flow rate. As we have shown previously [10], a full resolution of all oligomers in a diblock copolymer of ethylene oxide and caprolactone could be achieved in 5 – 6 hours.

## **6.2.  Introduction** 

Amphiphilic polymers, which contain hydrophilic and hydrophobic blocks, are in widespread use as surfactants or emulsifiers for various applications. In most of these polymers, the hydrophilic blocks consist of polyoxyethylene; while there is a wide variety in the hydrophobic part of these molecules [1,2]. 

Typical examples are fatty alcohol ethoxylates (FAE), which are synthesized by ethoxylation of fatty alcohols. Similar molecules can also be obtained by ethoxylation of alkyl phenols of fatty esters [3], especially the methyl esters (FAME). If these samples are synthesized from pure starting materials, they can be characterized quite easily [4-8]: the required information is the molar mass distribution of the ethoxylates and the content of polyethylene glycol (PEG), which results from chain transfer to water. With technical starting materials, characterization is more complicated: as (for example) technical dodecanol typically contains also tetradecanol and hexadecanol, the resulting ethoxylate will contain several series with different end groups [9]. 

The situation is even more complicated with fatty esters of polyethylene glycol (PEG), which can be synthesized by ethoxylation of fatty acids or esterification of PEG. Obviously, these products will not only contain the monoesters, but also PEG and the diesters, which makes characterization more difficult [8,10-12]. 

An even higher degree of complexity is found in fatty esters of ethoxylated sorbitan, which are often referred to as polysorbates (trade name TWEEN) [13-22]. Polysorbates are synthesized from sorbitol by subsequent ethoxylation and esterification with fatty acids. In the synthesis of polysorbates, various side reactions may occur, which lead to different by products (Scheme 1). 

At the temperature required for the ethoxylation, sorbitol undergoes a condensation to sorbitan (a tetrafunctional molecule), and to some extent a further condensation to isosorbide (a difunctional molecule). These molecules react with ethylene oxide (EO): sorbitan yields a four-armed star, isosorbide a linear chain. The water formed in the condensation reacts with ethylene oxide to yield PEG (also a linear. difunctional chain). These three hydrophilic species are then esterified with a fatty acid: obviously, this will not only yield the desired monoesters, but also some diesters of PEG and ethoxylated isosorbide as well as di-, tri- and even tetraesters of ethoxylated sorbitan. Moreover, the products will contain the unreacted ethoxylates. For the use as emulsifiers, the ethoxylates without the fatty ester are useless, while the higher esters are very hydrophobic; hence they

rather require an emulsifier. Obviously, the formation of side products in the synthesis cannot be completely avoided.

The key parameter for the application of amphiphilic molecules is the hydrophiliclipophilic balance (HLB), which depends on the relative length of the hydrophilic and lipophilic blocks, and - of course - on their number. This means, that possible side products with a different structure will strongly influence the physical properties and thus the performance of these products. Consequently, a reliable characterization of such products is crucial for many applications. 

The analysis of such complex samples is, however, a difficult task, which can only be solved by sophisticated techniques, such as two-dimensional chromatography [9,10,23,24] or matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight mass spectroscopy (MALDITOF-MS) [25]. While FAE can be reasonably characterized by MALDI-TOF-MS alone, this is not the case with highly complex polymers such as polysorbates, which may contain species with the same molar mass, but different structure. As will be shown in another 

115 

Chapter 6                                                       Characterization by Comprehensive 2D-HPLC 

paper [26], unambiguous results for such highly complex samples can only be obtained with fractions from liquid chromatography, in which all structures except one can be excluded. For example, there may be molar masses, which could correspond to PEG, the ethoxylates of sorbitan and isosorbide and their mono- and diesters with different fatty acids. In a spectrum of the raw material no discrimination is possible, but in a fraction containing just one of these series the other species cannot be present, and thus the assignment will unambiguous. 

In principle, different chromatographic modes can be employed for this purpose. 

## **6.2.1. Separation modes in liquid chromatography of polymers** 

Size exclusion chromatography (SEC), which is an entropic process, separates according to molecular size. In two-dimensional chromatography (2D-LC) of polymers, SEC may be applied, but because of its low selectivity is not the method of choice in the analysis of highly complex samples. 

Liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) is based on the interaction of the sample molecules with the stationary phase, which is typically governed by the enthalpy change. LAC can be performed on normal or reversed phases: NP-LAC separates according to the number of polar groups (EO, OH…), and RP-LAC separates according to the number of non-polar groups (alkyl, fatty ester etc.). 

In both cases, the retention is also influenced by the other structural units, consequently, a big mess of peaks will be observed, which originate from the different homologous series. This can be avoided at the critical point of adsorption (CAP) for a given structural unit: in a special mobile phase composition (and temperature) entropic and enthalpic contributions compensate each other. In this case the corresponding polymer chain becomes “chromatographically invisible”, which means, that all (non-functional) chains elute at the same volume, regardless their molar mass. 

This is utilized in Liquid Chromatography at Critical Conditions (LCCC) [12,27-30]. If a polymer contains an end group, which is adsorbed at the CAP of the repeat unit, these chains will elute as a sharp peak (independent on molar mass) at an elution volume corresponding to that of the end group [31]. Under such conditions, monofunctionals and A-B diblocks (with an adsorbing block A) elute at the CAP for B as narrow peaks, and so do B-A-B triblocks. 

116 

Chapter 6                                                       Characterization by Comprehensive 2D-HPLC 

A completely different behaviour is, however, observed with A-B-A triblocks (or difunctional chains) [11,28]. The probability of the second end group (or block) to be adsorbed depends on the size of the block between them: even at the CAP for B, the retention of A-B-A structures decreases with increasing length of the block B. Consequently, the individual oligomers will be separated in SEC order, but at elution volumes much larger than the void volume. This can be utilized for the separation of diesters [6,8]. 

On the other hand, small amounts of diesters will be difficult to detect, if they are resolved into many very small peaks. In two-dimensional separations, this is an even more serious problem: in the second dimension such small peaks will often be below the detection limit. Especially difficult is the situation with amphiphilic stars, such as the higher esters in polysorbates. A separation according to the number of hydrophobic groups at the CAP for PEG is rather difficult in such complex samples. As is shown in another paper [22], the hydrophilic part and the monoesters can be resolved quite well, but the higher esters cannot easily be quantified, as they do not elute as single peaks. 

In this paper we have now tried to find a system, which allows a separation of amphiphilic molecules according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups. This would mean, that the separation according to the size of the centre block in A-B-A structures must be suppressed, which should not be possible, if the separation is based on the adsorption on the surface of the stationary phase. The desired retention behaviour might, however, be observed, if the separation was based on partitioning, as is the case in hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) [32-35]. 

As will be shown in other papers [36,37], a separation of linear and branched ethoxylates without hydrophobic groups (such as PEG, their mono- and dimethyl ethers, PEG macromonomers, ethoxylated glycerol etc.) according to the number of hydroxy groups can be achieved without any influence of the number of EO units. Hence we have tried to apply this concept also to amphiphilic molecules.

## **6.3.2.  Methanolysis of polysorbates** 

Methanolysis of polysorbates was performed in 2-5 ml (filling volume) mirowave process vials (Biotage AB), which were oven dried under vacuum prior to use. The samples were dissolved in the twenty-fold volume of methanol, to which 0.0005g sodium had been added [22] . These solutions were added to the vials which were subsequently sealed with an aluminum crimp top and Teflon septum.The vials were then evacuated and purged with argon (3 cycles). The sealed microwave process vials were subsequently introduced into the cavity of the single-mode microwave reactor (Biotage Initiator Eight EXP 2.0, absorbance level: high). The reaction temperature was 100°C. The reaction time required for complete methanolysis (as confirmed by LCCC) was 10 min [22]. 

## **6.3.3.  Synthesis of esters** 

Mono and diesters of PEG and higher esters of polysorbates were synthesized by conventional heating in toluene with p-toluene sulphonic acid as catalyst. Water was removed by physical means such as distillation as low boiling azeotropes with toluene using a Dean-Stark apparatus. For synthesis of mono and diesters of PEG, molar ratio of alcohol and fatty acid was approximately 1:2 respectively. The reaction was completed in 5-6 hours at 150°C. Both mono and diesters are obtained in reasonable amounts. Higher esters of polysorbates were synthesized in the same way with the corresponding molar ratio.

## **6.5. Conclusions** 

Polysorbates can be characterized by comprehensive two-dimensional liquid chromatography with a combination of a reversed phase column and a HILIC column. In principle, the order of dimensions can be changed, but in practice it is favorable to use the reversed phase column in the first dimension, as the higher esters have rather high elution volumes, which would not allow to finish a chromatogram within a reasonable cycle time. On the HILIC column, all peaks appear in a sufficiently short time, which allows cycle times of 2 minutes. Under such conditions, a full separation of the different functionalities in polysorbates can be performed in 90 minutes. Further optimization with shorter columns in both dimensions and smaller sample loops should reduce the cycle time to 1 min and the total analysis time to less than one hour.

## **Separation of polysorbates by liquid chromatography on a HILIC column and identification of peaks by MALDITOF-MS** 

## **7.1.  Abstract** 

Polysorbates can be separated according to their functionality and architecture by liquid chromatography on a HILIC column in acetone – water mobile phases containing 90 to 97 % acetone. The different polymer homologous series are separated according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups and elute as narrow peaks. The individual fractions were identified by MALDI-TOF-MS.

## **7.2. Introduction** 

The key parameter for the application of amphiphilic molecules is the hydrophiliclipophilic balance (HLB), which depends on the relative length of the hydrophilic and lipophilic blocks, and - of course - on their number. This means, that possible side products with a different structure will strongly influence the physical properties and thus the performance of these products. The formation of such side products in the synthesis can, however, not be completely avoided. Especially complex mixtures are found in polysorbates (Tweens) [1-11], as is shown in Figure 1. Polysorbates are synthesized from sorbitol by subsequent ethoxylation and esterification with fatty acids. At the high temperatures required for the ethoxylation, sorbitol undergoes a condensation to sorbitan (a tetrafunctional molecule), and to some extent a further condensation to isosorbide (a difunctional molecule). The water formed in the condensation reacts with ethylene oxide to yield PEG (also a difunctional chain). These three hydrophilic species are now esterified with a fatty acid: obviously, this will not only yield the desired monoester of ethoxylated sorbitan (1), but also the monoesters of ethoxylated isosorbide (2) and PEG (3) as well as some diesters of PEG and ethoxylated isosorbide as well as di-, tri- and even tetraesters of ethoxylated sorbitan. Moreover, the products will contain the unreacted ethoxylates. For the use as emulsifiers, the ethoxylates without the fatty ester are useless, while the higher esters ar very hydrophobic, hence they rather require an emulsifier. 

Obviously, the analysis of such complex samples can only be solved by sophisticated techniques, such as two-dimensional chromatography  [12-15], matrix-assisted laser desorption ionization time-of-flight mass spectroscopy (MALDI-TOF-MS) [3,4,16] and combinations of different chromatographic techniques with MALDI-TOF-MS [16]. For the chromatographic separation, different modes of chromatography may be applied. 

## **7.2.1.  Separation modes in liquid chromatography of polymers** 

Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) separates according to molecular size. In SEC, which is an entropic process, the interaction parameter is strongly negative. In twodimensional chromatography (2D-LC) of polymers, SEC may be used as the second dimension, but it does not make sense in the analysis of polysorbates by HPLC x MALDITOF MS. 

Liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) is based on the interaction of the sample molecules with the stationary phase, which is typically governed by the enthalpy change. 

133 

Chapter 7 

HILIC and MALDI TOF-MS 

LAC can be performed on normal or reversed phases: NP-LAC separates according to the number of polar groups (EO, OH..), and RP-LAC separates according to the number of non-polar groups (alkyl, fatty ester etc..). In both cases, the retention is also influenced by the other structural units, consequently, overlapping series of peaks will be observed, which originate from the different functionalities and architectures. 

For the separation of polymers with different functionality, Liquid Chromatography at Critical Conditions (LCCC) is the method of choice: in a special mobile phase composition (and temperature) entopic and enthalpic contributions compensate each other. At the socalled critical point of adsorption (CAP) for a given structural unit the corresponding polymer chain becomes “chromatographically invisible”, which means, that all chains elute at the same volume, regardless their molar mass [17-20]. 

If a polymer contains an end group, which is adsorbed at the CAP of the repeat unit, these chains will elute as a sharp peak (independent on molar mass) at an elution volume corresponding to that of the end group. Monofunctional molecules and A-B diblocks (with an adsorbing block A) elute at the CAP for B as narrow peaks, and so do B-A-B triblocks. A completely different elution pattern is, however, observed with A-B-A triblocks (or difunctional chains) [18,21]. The probability of the second adsorbing group (or block) depends on the size of the (critical) block between them: even at the CAP for B, the retention of A-B-A structures decreases with increasing length of the block B. Consequently, the individual oligomers will be separated in SEC order, but at elution volumes much larger than the void volume [15,21,22]. This can be utilized for the separation of diesters, but it is a serious drawback, if small amounts of diesters have to be detected, which would only be possible, if they elute as a reasonably narrow peak. In twodimensional separations, this is an even more serious problem: in the second dimension such small peaks will often be below the detection limit. 

Especially difficult is the situation with amphiphilic stars, such as the higher esters in polysorbates. A separation according to the number of hydrophobic groups at the CAP for PEG is rather difficult in such complex samples. As is shown in another paper  [11], the hydrophilic part and the monoesters can be resolved quite well, but the higher esters cannot easily be quantified, as they do not elute as single peaks. 

There is, however, also another option: in another paper  [23]we have described the separation of ethoxylates according to the number of hydroxy groups by Hydrophilic interaction chromatography (HILIC) is typically used for quite different purposes [24-27]. On a HILIC column in 95-97 % acetone-water a separation of PEG-esters and polysorbates 

134 

Chapter 7 

according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups could be realized [28]. This technique has also been applied in two-dimensional comprehensive chromatography of polysorbates [29]. In these separations, HILIC was combined with a reversed phase column in the other dimension. An unambiguous identification was, however, only possible for the peaks of PEG and its mono-and diesters, for which standards were available. Some other peaks could be identified by analogy: the methanolyzed polysorbates should contain molecules with four hydroxy groups (ethoxylated sorbitan) and others with two hydroxy groups (PEG and ethoxylated isosorbide). If only two peaks appear in the chromatogram of such a sample, the difunctionals should elute in the first peak and the tetrafunctionals in the second one. 

In this study we have now separated polysorbates on the HILIC column and collected fractions, which were subsequently analyzed by MALDI-TOF-MS. 

In a recent paper [3], Ayorinde has described the analysis of polysorbates by MALDITOF-MS, and found, bimodal distributions, in which the ethoxylated sorbitan contains twice the number of EO units than the ethoxylated isosorbide and PEG. This is absolutely reasonable, as the tetrafunctional sorbitan can grow in four directions, and the difunctional isosorbide and PEG only in two. Similar results were also obtained by Frison-Norrie  [4]. The spectra obtained with the fractions from the HILIC column are much easier to interprete than those of the raw samples. An overlay of these spectra was in excellent agreement with Ayorinde´s results. This helps to a better understanding of the spectra obtained with the raw samples. Furthermore it allows the interpretation of the results obtained by two-dimensional comprehensive liquid chromatography  [29].

## **7.3.2.    Methanolysis of polysorbates** 

Methanolysis of polysorbates was performed in 2-5 ml (filling volume) mirowave process vials (Biotage AB), which were oven dried under vacuum prior to use. The samples were dissolved in the twenty-fold volume of methanol, to which 0.0005g sodium had been added [11] . These solutions were added to the vials which were subsequently sealed with an aluminum crimp top and Teflon septum.The vials were then evacuated and purged with argon (3 cycles). The sealed microwave process vials were subsequently introduced into the cavity of the single-mode microwave reactor (Biotage Initiator Eight EXP 2.0, absorbance level: high). The reaction temperature was 100°C.The reaction time required for complete methanolysis (as confirmed by LCCC) was 10 min. 

## **7.3.3.  Synthesis of esters** 

Mono and diesters of PEG and higher esters of polysorbates were synthesized by conventional heating in toluene with p-toluene sulphonic acid as catalyst. Water was removed by physical means such as distillation as low boiling azeotropes with toluene using a Dean-Stark apparatus. For synthesis of mono and diesters of PEG, molar ratio of alcohol and fatty acid was approximately 1:2 respectively. The reaction was completed in 5-6 hours at 150°C. Both mono and diesters are obtained in reasonable amounts. Higher esters of polysorbates were synthesized in the same way with the corresponding molar ratio. 

## **7.3.4.  Chromatography** 

These investigations were performed using a gradient system Ultimate 3000, consisting of a pump DGP-3600A, solvent degasser SRD-3600, column thermostat TCC-3000, autosampler WPS-3000SL, all from Dionex (Germerink, Germany), an evaporative light scattering detector PL-ELS 2100 (Polymer Laboratories / Varian, Church Stretton, Shropshire, UK). All separations were performed at a flow rate of 0.5 ml/min. The mobile phases were mixed by the gradient pump, so their compositions are given in volume per cent. Data acquisition and processing was performed using the software Chromeleon (Dionex, Germerink, Germany).

## **7.3.5.  MALDI-TOF-MS** 

MALDI-TOF Mass Spectrometry was performed on a Micromass TofSpec 2E Time-ofFlight Mass Spectrometer. The instrument is equipped with a nitrogen laser (337 nm wavelength, operated at a frequency of 5 Hz), and a time lag focusing unit. Ions were generated by irradiation slightly above the threshold laser power. Positive ion spectra were recorded in reflectron mode applying an accelerating voltage of 20 kV and externally calibrated with a suitable mixture of poly(ethyleneglycol)s (PEG). The spectra of 100-150 shots were averaged to improve the signal-to-noise ratio. Analysis of data was done with MassLynx-Software V3.5 (Micromass/Waters, Manchester, UK). 

Sample solutions were prepared by mixing a solution of dithranol (c = 10mg/mL in THF), a solution of the analyte (c = 2 mg/mL in THF), and a solution of CF3COONa (NaTFA; c = 0.1 mg/mL in THF), in a ratio of 7:1:1 (v/v/v). 0.5 µL of the resulting mixture were deposited on the sample plate (stainless steel) and allowed to dry under air.

## **8.2. Introduction** 

Polyoxyethylene sorbitan esters (polysorbates) are important class of amphiphilic polymers, which are commonly used as surfactants, emulsifiers, and stabilizers in pharmaceuticals, food, cosmetic and personal care products. They have a number of other applications as well [1-4] 

Polysorbates are commercially available from various producers: Akzo, Auschem, Hefti, Henkel, and ICI in Europe; Chemax, Croda, Henkel/Emery, Hodag, ICI Americas, Karlshamns, Lipo Chemical, Lonza, Norman/Fox, PPG/Mazer, Stepan, and Witco in North America; Kao, Nikko Chemicals, Nippon Nyukazai, Nippon Oil and Fats, Sanyo, and Toho in Japan. Polysorbates are commercially available under the name Tweens (a registered trademark of Croda international). The estimated annual production capacity of sorbitan esters is 20,000 t/a [2]. 

Polysorbates are considered as fatty esters of ethoxylated sorbitan (which is obtained by dehydration of sorbitol) with approximately 20 ethylene oxide units per fatty acid. Polysorbates differ from each other in fatty alkyl chain length [2,5], namely lauric acid, palmitic acid, stearic acid and oleic acid in polysorbate 20, 40, 60 and 80, respectively. As the fatty acids used in the synthesis are typically technical grade, the polysorbates may contain other esters as well. They contain, however, always other structures, too (Figure 1): at the high temperatures used in the synthesis (as well for esterification and ethoxylation) further dehydration of sorbitan to isosorbide takes place, moreover, there may be alcoholysis and transesterifcation reactions. Consequently, polysorbates will contain ethoxylated sorbitan and isosorbide, polyethylene glycol (which results from the ethoxylation of water) as well as the mono-and diesters of ethoxylated isosorbide and PEG esters, the corresponding diesters and probably also some tri- and tetraesters of ethoxylated sorbitan. Obviously, this makes polysorbate quite complex in their composition. [6,7]. Tween 40 and Tween 60 are specified as monoesters of palmitic and stearic acid, respectively, however the may contain a number of different esters functionalities (depending on the purity of the fatty acid used as starting material), non-esterified hydrophilic ethoxylates and PEG, which are, of course, no surfactants.

Characterization of polysorbates by single MALDI TOF MS gives very complex spectra due to mass discrimination [10]. 

Different modes of liquid chromatography can be used for characterization of polymers: In Size exclusion chromatography (SEC), the separation is controlled by the entropy change: molecules are separated on the basis of their hydrodynamic volume. Larger molecules (which are partially excluded from the pores of the stationary phase) are eluted earlier than smaller ones [11-13]. Because of the low efficiency, SEC is not suitable for such a fractionation. Liquid Adsorption Chromatography (LAC) separates according to the number of groups, which may interact with the stationary phase. LAC is typically

governed by enthalpy. Depending on the chemistry of stationary phase and mobile phase, retention depends on the number of hydrophilic groups (on normal phase columns: NPLAC) or hydrophobic groups (on reversed phase columns: RP-LAC) [14,15]. In the case of highly heterogeneous samples, however, a full resolution cannot be achieved in one dimension: typically several superimposed series of peaks will be obtained. 

Liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) allows a separation according to groups different from the repeat unit. At the critical adsorption point (CAP) the contributions of entropy and enthalpy compensate each other, and the polymer chain becomes thus “chromatographically invisible”. At the CAP for polyethylene glycol, separation can be achieved according to the number and length of the ester functionalities [16-18].

The latter approach also offers two options: heart-cutting and comprehensive chromatography. All of these techniques have their advantages and disadvantages. As has been shown previously [21], a combination of HPLC and MALDI TOF MS is very successful in the characterization of polymers of complex architecture and high polydispersity [22-24]. 

For many applications, however, a simple – one-dimensional – separation would yield a satisfactory result, if it yields information on the content of the individual functionalities: ethoxylates without a fatty acid, monoesters, diesters, and probably some higher esters. In this study, such a simple method for the separation of polysorbates should be developed, which allows a rapid characterization of polysorbates according to their functionality.

## **8.5.  Conclusions** 

Polysorbates can be analyzed by liquid chromatography at critical conditions for PEG in different mobile phases. In binary mobile phases it is not possible to separate the relevant components (ethoxylates of sorbitan and isosorbide and PEG and the corresponding monoand diesters from each other. The individual peaks can be identified by preparative HPLC and analysis of the fractions by MALDI-TOF-MS. Using gradient elution from critical conditions in a ternary mobile phase (methanol – acetone – water) to a CAP in a binary mobile phase (acetone – water) quite well resolved chromatograms with reasonably sharp peaks can be obtained. This allows a characterization of polysorbates by a simple onedimensional method.

## **Summary and Conclusions** 

This thesis deals with the characterization of functional and amphiphilic polymers of complex architecture. Polymeric materials with special properties can be obtained by introduction of structural units different from the repeat unit (such as other monomer units or functional end groups). The purpose of such modifications can be different: functional end groups may allow further reactions of the polymer (such as chain extension etc.), copolymers containing more than one kind of repeat unit may combine the properties of the homopolymers to yield a product with entirely new physical properties. In the case of copolymers, the arrangement of the individual segments or blocks has a very strong influence on the physical properties of the product. Functional polymers are often used as precursors for polymers with a special architecture. This requires defined end groups, which can undergo condensation, addition or polymerization reactions. Telechelics are linear chains with two identical end groups. For example, polyethylene glycol (PEG) is a linear chain with two hydroxy end groups, which can be converted to ester or urethane groups. 

The first part of the thesis is the characterization of functional polymers and oligomers with special end groups. This includes the characterization of Polyethylene glycol (PEG), their monomethyl ethers (PEG MME), Polyethylene glycol mono methacrylates (PEG MMA) and Polyethylene glycol dimethacrylates (PEG DMA) etc. Polyoxyethylenes with different functionality and architecture were separated according to the number of their terminal hydroxy groups on polar stationary phases in acetone water mobile phases containing 90 –97 % acetone. The best results were obtained on a HILIC column. Polyoxyethylene macromonomers were analyzed by one-dimensional liquid chromatography using different separation conditions, depending on the required information. These samples may contain polyethylene glycol (PEG) and the corresponding di(meth)acrylate besides the desired mono(meth)acrylate. The molar mass distribution (MMD) of the PEG and the monoester was obtained by liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC) on a reversed phase column in acetone – water with a gradient from 10 to 20 % acetone. The MMD of the diesters was achieved with isocratic elution by liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) on a reversed phase column in 31 % acetone, or using size exclusion (SEC) conditions for PEG and LAC conditions for the end groups. The absolute amount of the series with different functionality was obtained by critical conditions of separation in ternary mobile of acetone, methanol, and water along 

169 

the critical adsorption line (CAL). Under such conditions, all series elute as narrow peaks (regardless their MMD), which can easily be integrated and quantified. 

The second part of the thesis is the characterization of amphiphilic polymers with complex architecture, such as Polysorbates, which are commercially available under the name Tween. Tweens are complex mixtures of different functionalities. Molar mass distribution and chemical composition of different Tween samples (20, 40, 60 and 65) were determined by size exclusion chromatography (SEC) with dual detection. A separation of different functionalities was accomplished using liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC) on typical reversed phase columns with methanol – water and acetone - water as mobile phase. The size of the hydrophilic part was determined after microwave-assisted methanolysis, which allowed the identification of the fatty acids and functionalities of the hydrophilic cores. 

Complex polymer samples were characterized by combinations of different separation modes of HPLC in offline and online two dimensional separation : liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC), liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC), and liquid exclusion-adsorption chromatography (LEAC). Fatty acid ethoxylates, Polysorbates and fatty esters of polyethylene glycol (PEG) were analyzed by two-dimensional liquid chromatography (2D-LC) using liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) in the first and liquid exclusion adsorption chromatography (LEAC) as the second dimension. A full separation of all oligomers to the baseline was achieved in both dimensions. In twodimensional separations, the offline approach was compared to comprehensive chromatography, and the scope and limitations of both techniques were discussed 

Amphiphilic polymers – such as polysorbates and fatty esters of polyethylene glycol were analyzed by comprehensive two-dimensional liquid chromatography using a reversed phase column (under critical conditions for the polyoxyethylene chain) and a HILIC column, which may arrange in different order. As the retention of higher esters on the reversed phase column is very strong, this column was used as the first dimension. All the fractions elute within reasonable time on HILIC column. Polysorbates were separated according to their functionality and architecture by liquid chromatography on a HILIC column in acetone – water mobile phases. The different polymer homologous series were separated according to the number of terminal hydroxy groups and elute as narrow peaks. Different peaks were assigned by comparison with standards of known functionality, however the reliable identification of functionalities cannot be done alone by HPLC. The separated fractions were identified by Matrix assisted lasar desorption time of flight mass 

170 

spectroscopy (MALDI TOF-MS). The presence of various higher esters along with different hydrophilic functionalities was confirmed by MALDI TOF-MS. 

Thus the separation and characterization of functional and amphiphilic polymers of special architecture has been efficiently achieved by different modes of liquid chromatography as offline and online 2D-LC separation. Combination of HPLC and MALDI TOF-MS for characterization of polysorbates has been proved to resolve the complexity of such samples successfully.
