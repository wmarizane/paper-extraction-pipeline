## Detection limits of matrix-assisted laser desorption/ – ionisation mass spectrometry coupled to chromatography a new application of solvent-free sample preparation 

## Jana Falkenhagen and Steffen M. Weidner* 

Federal Institute for Materials Research and Testing (BAM), Unter den Eichen 87, 12205 Berlin, Germany 

Received 22 August 2005; Revised 24 October 2005; Accepted 24 October 2005 

The detection limits of matrix-assisted laser desorption/ionisation mass spectrometry (MALDI-MS) by semi-online coupling with chromatography were investigated using various mixtures of polyethylene oxides (PEOs) with different end groups. In contrast to the common ‘dried-droplet’ sample preparation technique, which results in an inhomogeneous sample-to-matrix ratio within the MALDI spot, the used coupling technique offers a very high reproducibility combined with surpassing sensitivity of a few femtograms over a broad range of sample-to-matrix ratios. These results are in correlation with the results of the recently established solvent-free MALDI-TOFMS method utilising the grinding approach and are also of assistance towards the more theoretical aspect of MALDI that suggests that there is no necessity for an analyte incorporation into a matrix crystal for excellent matrix-assistance. Recently, the principle of solvent-free sample preparation was introduced by Trimpin et al.[1–3] This involves the mixing of sample and matrix components by intense grinding in a mortar or, more efficiently, in a mill without any solvent. Primarily developed for the matrix-assisted laser desorption/ ionisation mass spectrometric (MALDI-MS) investigation of insoluble samples this method has become increasingly useful for the analysis of common synthetic and biopolymers.[4–6] In contrast to solvent-based preparation techniques an intense grinding of samples and matrix results in a very homogeneous powder downward to micrometer scale. A segregation based on different solubility of the both compounds in the solvent can be avoided. This leads to a very high spot-to-spot reproducibility. Finally, the ratio of sample and matrix in the mixture seems to be less crucial for a good spectra quality.[7] 

The latter reason in particular is extremely important for coupling chromatography with MALDI-MS. This method combination is very important for a MALDI analysis of broad distributed polymer samples and for a two-dimensional (2D) analysis of heterogeneous polymers.[8–10] The sample is sprayed onto the MALDI target by means of an interface. The solvent is evaporated in a nitrogen stream using a heated needle. However, the sample concentration within the chromatographic peak is not constant, i.e. very low at the flanks as well as comparatively high at the top of the peak. In contrast to that, the matrix solution is sprayed with a constant concentration onto the target before the sample is deposited or simultaneously with the sample. Therefore, different sample-to-matrix ratios are obtained, which strongly differ

from optimal values. This drawback can be overcome by adapting the matrix concentration to the peak intensity (e.g. controlling of the matrix pump flow by the outlet signal of an additional concentration detector). 

Additionally, the use of a micro-high-performance liquid chromatography (m-HPLC) system should be investigated. On the one hand, applying such a system, the amount of solvent to be evaporated can be drastically reduced. One the other hand, the absolute amount of sample deposited onto the MALDI target strongly decreases too. 

Our aim was to establish such conditions of sample deposition that can be compared to the ‘solvent-free’ sample preparation method. This could be achieved when the solvent is completely evaporated before the sample molecules impinge the matrix on the pre-coated target. 

The influence of various matrix-to-sample ratios on the spectra quality and sensitivity using these optimised conditions was investigated.

## MALDI-MS 

A Bruker Reflex III MALDI mass spectrometer (BrukerDaltonik, Bremen, Germany) operating at 20 kV acceleration voltage was used. For ionisation/desorption a UV laser working at a wavelength of 337 nm was applied. The laser pulse length was 3 ns. Typically, 100–200 transients were accumulated for one spectra. The mass spectrometer wascalibrated using different PEO standards. There was no additional salt added. 

The preparation of MALDI spots according to the ‘drieddroplet’ method was performed using premixed solutions: 2 mg of solid matrix were dissolved in 1 mL tetrahydrofuran (THF). Two different matrices – trihydroxyanthracene (dithranol) and trans-2-(3-(4-tert-butylphenyl)-2-methyl-2propenylidene)malonitrile (DCTB) – were used. A volume of 20 mL of each matrix solution was mixed with 20 mL of the polymer solution. For the preparation of spots, 1 mL of the mixture was deposited on the target by means of a pipette. After evaporation of the solvent the target was inserted into the mass spectrometer.

## Coupling of MALDI-MS with chromatography 

A commercially available interface (LC 500, LabConnection, USA) was used for semi-online coupling of liquid chromatography and mass spectrometry. 

In the first step, the target was coated with matrix by spraying using a secondary pump (Knauer, Germany). The principle of sample deposition is shown in Fig. 1. 1,8,9Trihydroxyanthracene (dithranol), 2,4,6-trihydroxyacetophenone (THAP) and trans-2-(3-(4-tert-butylphenyl)-2methyl-2-propenylidene)malonitrile (DCTB) dissolved in THF (ca. 1 mg/mL) were used. The deposition flow rate was 0.4 mL/min. The solvent was evaporated in a nitrogen gas stream under pressure at an elevated temperature of 1308C. The applied gas pressure was 20 psi which corresponds to a nitrogen gas flow rate of 2 L/min. 

Afterwards, the eluate of a chromatographic run was sprayed onto the pre-coated target. In order to avoid crystallisation of matrix on the tip of the sprayer needle the temperature had to be carefully adjusted. Due to the amount of water in the solvent mixture of system 2 the evaporation temperature (1508C) as well as the gas stream (30 psi ¼ 3 L/min) had to be increased to achieve a complete evaporation.

## Comparison of hand spotting (‘dried-droplet’ method) and spraying 

For a comparison of the hand spotting and the spraying methods the above mentioned mixture of PEO 1000 and 6000 was used as well. Figure 5 shows MALDI mass spectra of matrix/ sample mixtures with different ratios. A ratio of 10:1 (matrix/ sample) results in spectra with high resolution for both polymers. However, just a moderate increase in the amount of matrix up to a ratio of 100:1 leads to deterioration of the spectra quality. Although the intensity of signals remains more or less unaffected, the signal-to-noise ratio in the PEO 6000 region is much lower. In contrast to the PEO 1000 signal, which can be detected up to a matrix/sample ratio of 1000:1, a further increase in the amount of matrix in the mixture causes a disappearance of PEO 6000 peaks. At a higher ratio of 5000:1 neither PEO 1000 nor PEO 6000 signals can be detected in sufficient quality. 

Spraying was performed using the LC 500 interface. Single spots were created, i.e. the MALDI target did not rotate. The results are shown in Fig. 6. In contrast to the ‘dried-droplet’ method spectra of PEO 1000 and 6000 could be easily recorded using a mixture ratio of 5000:1. Surprisingly, even at a ratio of 50000:1 (matrix/sample), PEO 1000 signals could be detected. Obviously, the spraying method seems to be up to 100 times more effective in the 1000 Da range and 10 times more effective in higher mass regions.
