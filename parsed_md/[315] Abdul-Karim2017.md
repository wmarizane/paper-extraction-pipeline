## Synthesis and Characterization of Novel Biodegradable Di- and Tri-Block Copolymers Based on Ethylene Carbonate Polymer as Hydrophobic Segment 

## Rubina Abdul-Karim, Syed Ghulam Musharraf, Muhammad Imran Malik 

H.E.J. Research Institute of Chemistry, International Center for Chemical and Biological Sciences (ICCBS), University of Karachi, Karachi 75270, Pakistan 

Correspondence to: M. I. Malik (E-mail: mimran.malik@iccs.edu) 

Received 24 January 2017; accepted 21 February 2017; published online in Wiley Online Library DOI: 10.1002/pola.28559 

ABSTRACT: Synthesis of novel amphiphilic biodegradable block copolymers based on ethylene carbonate is reported in this study. Polyethylene glycol monomethyl ether (MeO-PEO) and polyethylene glycol (PEG) of varying molar masses are used as macro-initiator for ring-opening polymerization of ethylene carbonate in the presence of sodium stannate trihydrate as a heterogeneous transesterification catalyst. Earlier elution of block copolymer from macro-initiator in size exclusion chromatography (SEC) indicated the successful synthesis of the block copolymers. Ratios of both types of blocks are varied systematically. Liquid chromatography at critical conditions is used for the analysis of the non-critical individual blocks, and if there 

are any critical segments that are not attached to the noncritical block. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first report on the synthesis of ethylene carbonate-based amphiphilic block copolymers. Chromatographic critical conditions of the ethylene carbonate polymer are also reported for the first time. VC 2017 Wiley Periodicals, Inc. J. Polym. Sci., Part A: Polym. Chem. 2017, 55, 1887–1893 

KEYWORDS: amphiphilic block copolymers; critical adsorption point of poly(ethylene ether-carbonate); liquid chromatography at critical conditions; poly(ethylene ether-carbonate) 

> INTRODUCTION Search of novel and efficient drug delivery systems is one of the hot research topics of current science. An inherent part of drug delivery and other medical-related formulations are biocompatible, biodegradable, or bioresorbable polymers.[1,2] Moderate rate of bio-degradation and non-toxic degradation products of aliphatic polycarbonates make them a captivating candidate for the above-mentioned applications.[3,4] 

Ring-opening of cyclic carbonates is the most suitable method for the synthesis of fairly high molar mass polycarbonates.[5,6] Block copolymers containing polycarbonate segments have been reported by several scientists in order to induce peculiar slow degradation and other mechanical properties.[7–9] Amphiphilic biodegradable block copolymers are very important materials. Their amphiphilic behaviour, mechanical, and physical properties can be manipulated by varying relative block lengths, molar mass, and chemical composition of the blocks, for required applications.[10,11] 

Polyethylene oxide is the most widely used hydrophilic segment of synthetic biodegradable block copolymers. It is nontoxic, biocompatible and is easily excreted from body through urinary system.[12–14] The block copolymers of PEO 

with polycarbonates are alluring candidate for medicalrelated applications due to their peculiar biodegradability, slow degradation rates, and non-acid degradation products. Amphiphilic behaviour of the block copolymers can be tuned by changing the type of the carbonate segment and/or the relative lengths and composition of both blocks. There are several literature reports on the synthesis of polycarbonatebased amphiphilic block copolymers.[15–17] In abovementioned reports, poly(trimethylene carbonate), poly(2,2dimethylene carbonate), poly(5-methyl-5-allyloxycarbonylpropylene carbonate), and poly(5-allyloxy-1,3-dioxane-2-one) have been used as hydrophobic block. Hydrophobicity of the polycarbonate block can be tailored by inducing different pendant groups such as OH, NH2, and COOH on the polymer chain, that can be further used for grafting.[17,18] In none of the above-mentioned literature, polymer synthesized from ethylene carbonate monomer is used as hydrophobic segment. In this study, we would like to report first-time synthesis of amphiphilic block copolymers containing ethylene carbonate-based polymer as hydrophobic block.

Critical adsorption point of PEEC is also reported for the first time and is used for the analysis of the novel biodegradable block copolymers. 

## HPLC OF POLYMERS 

Currently, three liquid chromatographic methods are mostly utilized to characterize polymers, namely, size exclusion chromatography (SEC), Interaction liquid chromatography (iLC) or liquid adsorption chromatography (LAC), and liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC). 

SEC is the simplest, and most widely used technique for characterization of synthetic and natural polymers.[41,42] Separations by SEC are entropy controlled and depend on the hydrodynamic volume of the analyte in dilute solution. It is not possible to separate polymers with similar hydrodynamic volume in solution but varying in architecture, chemical composition, or topology. Polymers elute in order of decreasing hydrodynamic volume. SEC can be used as a first test for 

the augmentation in the hydrodynamic size of the polymers in solution that is related to the relative molar mass by external calibration, however, SEC cannot provide any information about chemical composition or if the polymer sample is a mixture of two types of homopolymers or all polymer segments are attached to each other making block copolymer. 

For complex polymeric systems, interaction modes of liquid chromatography reveal more information because separation could be achieved with regard to the interaction strength of the analyte with the stationary phase. These modes are particularly useful for determination of unwanted homopolymers in the copolymer samples. Interaction chromatographic modes can be isocratic or gradient including both solvent and temperature gradients.[29–32,43–46] 

Larger polymers are strongly adsorbed, and consequently are held longer, while smaller polymers are only slightly adsorbed and elute earlier. Interaction liquid chromatography (iLC) has better resolution than SEC, however, it is not as universal as SEC. For a specific polymer, the strength of the enthalpic interaction has to be controlled to accomplish a reproducible separation. Selection of a suitable stationary phase, mobile phase, and gradient (either solvent or temperature) is not universal and often requires extensive experimentation. The recovery of the polymers, and in turn their quantitation is also influenced by events such as precipitation of the polymers in the injector, breakthrough peaks, and irreversible adsorption of large polymers.[43,47,48] At the intersection of exclusion (entropy controlled) and interaction modes (enthalpy controlled) of liquid chromatography of polymers, there is a special combination of mobile phase, stationary phase, temperature and analyte, where all the polymers elute at the same elution volume independent of their molar mass. This special point is called critical adsorption point (CAP) and this mode of liquid chromatography of polymers is termed as liquid chromatography under critical conditions (LCCC).[29–32] 

LCCC effectively allows separation of complex polymers with regard to distributions other than molar mass such as functionality,[29–32,36–40] and the other block.[24–35] In LCCC, exclusion and adsorption effects counterbalance each other, and critical homopolymers are transported along the column become ‘chromatographically invisible’, which means that their elution is no more dependent on the molar mass. Thus, in the case of a block copolymer, one block can be eluted under critical conditions while the other block can elute in either exclusion[28–32,36,49] or adsorption mode.[28–36] This allows separation of one of the parent homopolymers from the block copolymer, and separation of block copolymer is achieved with regard to the length of the non-critical block. The limitations of LCCC are the same as discussed for interaction modes of LC of polymers such as limited recovery, irreversible adsorption etc. Another limitation of LCCC-SEC is decreased accuracy of the molar mass distribution of noncritical block with the increasing length of the critical block, nonetheless, it provides reasonable estimates.[25]

## RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 

The polymerization of ethylene carbonate by sodium stannate trihydrate as a heterogeneous transesterification catalyst, and the relative content of the carbonate to ether linkages have been described in our recent publication, through comprehensive structural analysis by advanced NMR techniques.[19] The coordination ring-opening polymerization of ethylene carbonate can be initiated by hydroxyl endgroup. The hydroxyl end-group of polyethylene glycols (macro-initiator) is used as initiating moiety for polymerization of ethylene carbonate to synthesize di- and tri-block copolymers (see Scheme 1). There can be expulsion of carbon dioxide during polymerization that result in ether linkages in the polymer block based on ethylene carbonate. Nonetheless, the ratio of ether and carbonate linkages remains around one under all conditions. Therefore, both blocks shall have quite different properties from each other and products can show peculiar self-assembly behaviour. The specific hydrophilic– lipophilic balance of these biodegradable amphiphilic block copolymers can be exploited for novel applications. The hydrophilic–lipophilic balance can be further tailored by varying block lengths and compositions of the both segments. In this study, lengths of both blocks are varied

## CONCLUSION 

Synthesis of di- and tri-block amphiphiles based on poly(ethylene oxide) as hydrophilic block and poly(ethylene ethercarbonate) segment as hydrophobic part is reported for the first time. Block copolymers are characterized by liquid chromatography at critical conditions. Critical conditions of PEG have been reported by several authors. However, the critical adsorption point of hydrophobic segment PEEC is never reported in literature. The hydrophobic segment also contains ether linkages alongside targeted carbonate linkages. The statistical nature of the hydrophobic segment as revealed by NMR analysis[19] is endorsed by chromatography. Statistical nature of the segment allowed for the establishment of critical adsorption point. The analysis of the individual molar mass of non-critical block, and presence of free hydrophilic or hydrophobic critical segments is monitored by liquid chromatography at critical conditions. It has been shown that in case of lesser amount of ethylene carbonate monomer in the sample, some of the macro-initiator remains unreacted. However, with increased amount of ethylene carbonate in the reaction mixture all the macro-initiators are initiated, making a part of block copolymer.
