# Separation of all oligomers in polyethylene glycols and their monomethyl ethers by one-dimensional liquid chromatography 

Bernd Trathnigg & Hasnat Ahmed 

Received: 26 April 2010 /Revised: 18 June 2010 /Accepted: 20 June 2010 /Published online: 8 July 2010 # Springer-Verlag 2010 

Abstract Different polymer homologous series having the same repeat unit, but different end groups, can be separated by one-dimensional LAC according to the number of repeat units and functionality, if a favorable combination of the interaction parameters of the repeat unit and the end groups can be found. As an example, polyethylene glycol (PEG) can be determined in PEG monomethyl ethers. The molar mass distribution of the minor component in such samples can be determined even at concentrations of a few percent. 

Keywords Polyethylene glycol[. ] Monomethyl ether[.] Functionaliy[. ] Liquid adsorption chromatography[.] Interaction parameter 

## Introduction 

The presence of chains having a different functionality and their determination is a pertinent problem in polymer chemistry. For some applications, small amounts of such impurities can be negligible, but even traces of chains with different functionality may have a dramatic effect on product quality. Polymers and oligomers with different functionality may be formed as side products by chain transfer reactions in the polymerization; they may, however, also originate from incomplete conversion in the modification of end groups.

On the other hand, chain transfer to the monomer may occur in the polymerization of propylene oxide, which results in the formation of chains with allylic end groups besides the desired diols [2–5]. If the diols are used as chain extenders (e.g., in the production of polyurethanes), monofunctional molecules will have an adverse effect on the properties of the final product. 

The same reactions can also occur in the synthesis of block copolymers: traces of water in the polyethylene glycol (PEG) used as initiator will result in the formation of homopolymers by a chain transfer reaction. 

If a chain with terminal hydroxy groups is esterified, one may expect unreacted starting material besides mono- and diesters. Depending on the application, this will have different consequences: Monoester of fatty acids with PEG can be used as emulsifier [6, 7]. Obviously, PEG has no emulsifying properties; hence, it is useless, but the diesters are highly hydrophobic and would even require an emulsifier to prevent precipitation. A much more dramatic effect must be expected in macromonomers, from which graft copolymers can be synthesized by copolymerization with other monomers: The macromonomers are typically synthesized by reaction of PEG–monomethyl ethers (MME) with methacryloyl chloride [8]. If the macromonomer contains diesters, these will act as crosslinker. 

A similar effect must be expected in the PEGylation of proteins and drugs etc. [9–15]. For this purpose, monomethyl ethers of PEG are typically used, which can be synthesized by polymerization of ethylene oxide with methanol or the monomethyl ether of triethylene glycol [8] as initiator and sodium hydroxide or a similar catalyst (such as potassium naphthalene [16]). As the molar concentration of chains is much more important than the weight concentration, a reliable characterization of functional polymers and oligomers with respect to functionality and molar mass is crucial for many applications. 

The question is, however, the suitability of the analytical methods. In literature, this has often been done by different chromatographic techniques, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR), and MALDI-TOF-MS [8, 16]. 

All these techniques have some limitations. For example, gas chromatography can be used only for separation of low molecular weight oligomers, and NMR yields only average values; moreover, it is not sensitive enough for the determination of low contents [17]. MALDI-TOF-MS can perfectly resolve all oligomers, but is problematic with respect to quantitation, especially in the low molecular range (close to the molar mass of the matrix). 

In principle, a separation of polymers according to functionality can be achieved by liquid chromatography at critical conditions (LCCC) [8, 18–21]. At a special mobile phase composition and temperature, the retention of a polymer chain is independent of molar mass. At this socalled critical point of adsorption (CAP), a separation according to functionality becomes possible. This technique works very well with strongly adsorbing end groups, as is the case with fatty alcohol ethoxylate or alkyl phenol ethoxylates, but it is difficult if the end groups and the repeat units are not sufficiently different. 

Such a situation occurs with PEG-MME: At the CAP for PEG on a reversed-phase column, the methoxy end group of PEG-MME is adsorbed very slightly. Nevertheless, such a separation has been described recently [22]. We have thus decided to concentrate on this system as a commercially important example. 

Besides the total amount of the by-product PEG (which can be obtained from LCCC), its molar mass distribution (MMD) is important as the amount of diols depends on the total amount of the PEG and its number average of molar mass. 

The molar mass of the PEG is also highly important for another reason: In the separation described by Barman, an evaporative light scattering detector (ELSD) has to be used, as PEG elutes with the solvent peak. With this detector, one has to take into account not only its nonlinear response but also the molar mass dependence of response factors [23]. Barman obtained this information by LC-MS, which is, however, not available in most labs. Moreover, quantification of small amounts is somewhat problematic in LC-MS. The better approach would be a separation of all oligomers of both series (glycols and monomethyl ethers) by chromatography. 

In principle, this is possible by two-dimensional liquid chromatography (2D-LC). As we have shown previously, a full separation of all oligomers of both homologous series can be achieved [24]. In this paper, we have described a new approach, which employs LCCC in the first dimension with heart-cutting of the PEG and PEG-MME fractions, which are subsequently analyzed by gradient LAC in the second dimension. The fractions are focused by adding water to the eluate from the first dimension (between the column and the switching valve) in order to make the solvent containing the fractions a weaker eluent than the mobile phase of the second dimension. This procedure works very well even at very low PEG content (less than 1%), but requires rather complex equipment. 

Another approach could be LAC under conditions, which allow a separation of the individual polymer homologous series without overlapping peaks. In this study, we wanted to find conditions, under which such a separation is possible, and—if so—which amounts of PEG could be identified and quantitatively determined. Moreover, this approach should be compared with the others mentioned above.

## Results and discussion 

First of all, we wanted to reproduce the LCCC separation described by Barman [22] with our equipment. Critical conditions for EO were found on the Synergi Fusion RP column in 82% methanol at a temperature of 25 °C. Under these conditions, the retention times of PEGs and PEGMMEs are sufficiently different to allow such a separation (not shown). An even better resolution can be achieved in acetone–water, as has been described in a previous paper [24]. 

After all, a separation of PEG and PEG-MME by LCCC can be achieved, but there remains the question of the MMD of PEG. As we have shown previously [24], this can be solved by 2D-LC, but this approach requires rather complex equipment, which is not available in many labs.

## Conclusion 

Different polymer homologous series with the same repeat unit, but different end groups, can be separated by LAC according to the number of repeat units and functionality by one-dimensional LAC, if a favorable combination of the interaction parameters of the repeat unit and the end groups can be realized. This can be achieved in principle by variation of mobile phase composition and temperature. If the dependencies of the interaction parameters of repeat unit and end groups are known on temperature and mobile phase composition is known, one may predict whether such a combination exists. The molar mass distribution of the minor component in such a mixture can be determined even at concentrations of a few percent. If a favorable combination of interaction parameters does not exist, two-dimensional separations with LCCC as the first and LAC as the second dimension have to be applied. In both cases, however, the crucial point in the determination of the MMD is the detector. In a separate paper [43], we shall show how accurate results for the MMD of the minor component can be obtained.
